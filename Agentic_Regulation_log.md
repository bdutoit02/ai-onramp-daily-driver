# Process Log

**Research question**: "What are the three most significant regulatory changes affecting digital skills requirements in South African banking since January 2024?"

---

## Step 1: Regulatory Landscape Scan
**Agent type**: Researcher
**Task assigned**: Identify all notable regulatory changes, directives, guidelines, and policy updates affecting the South African banking sector issued since January 2024. Focus on SARB, PA, FSCA, FIC, National Treasury, and relevant legislation. For each change, record: name/title, issuing body, effective date, brief description, and any technology/digital component.
**Key findings**: The agent identified over 30 distinct regulatory changes across multiple bodies, grouped into 8 categories:
- **SARB/PA**: Basel III reforms (effective July 2025), FRTB/CVA roadmap, model risk management focus, third-party risk/BaaS guidance, climate risk guidance, CoDI launch (April 2024), cloud computing consultation, NPS reform, PA Regulatory Strategy 2025-2030
- **Joint Standards (PA + FSCA)**: IT Governance and Risk Management (Joint Standard 1 of 2023, effective Nov 2024), Cybersecurity and Cyber Resilience (Joint Standard 2 of 2024, effective June 2025), Cloud Computing guidance (Joint Communication 2 of 2025), AI in Financial Sector report (Nov 2025)
- **FSCA**: Open Finance position paper (March 2024), CASP licensing (ongoing), regulation plans
- **FIC**: Guidance Note 7A on AML/CFT compliance, Directive 9 (crypto travel rule, effective April 2025)
- **National Treasury/Legislation**: AML Amendment Bill, FATF grey list exit (Oct 2025), Climate Change Act (commenced March 2025), COFI Bill, NPS Bill, NCA amendment regulations
- **Information Regulator**: Amended POPIA Regulations (April 2025), escalated enforcement
- **IFWG**: Stablecoin landscape diagnostic, regulatory sandbox

**Decision**: Proceed to Sub-task 2 — research the specific digital skills implications of these regulatory changes. The landscape scan is thorough and provides a strong foundation. The most digitally significant changes appear to be: (1) Joint Standard on IT Governance, (2) Joint Standard on Cybersecurity, (3) AI report/emerging AI governance, (4) Cloud computing guidance, (5) Open Finance framework, (6) Basel III data/systems requirements, and (7) POPIA amendments. The next researcher should focus on the digital skills demands created by these.

---

## Step 2: Digital Skills Implications Research
**Agent type**: Researcher
**Task assigned**: For each of the 8 most digitally significant regulatory changes identified in Step 1, research: (1) what specific digital skills are now required, (2) who in the bank needs them, (3) whether formal training/certification is mandated, (4) compliance timelines, and (5) industry commentary on the skills gap. Also research BankSETA skills plans, BASA position papers, consulting firm reports, and university programmes developed to meet these requirements.
**Key findings**: The agent produced a detailed analysis for each regulatory change:
- **Joint Standard 1 (IT Governance)**: Requires IT governance frameworks (COBIT/ITIL/ISO 27001 alignment), IT risk management, business continuity skills. Board must approve IT strategy. IT head must have "requisite skills and experience." Comprehensive training programme mandated. Effective Nov 2024.
- **Joint Standard 2 (Cybersecurity)**: Requires cybersecurity strategy development, asset classification, DLP, incident response, threat monitoring. **Annual cybersecurity awareness training mandated for board and all staff.** CSIR survey: 63% of cyber roles unfilled, 35,000 vacancies nationally. Effective June 2025.
- **AI Report**: Banks lead AI adoption at 52% but 53% of staff lack sufficient AI training. 37-39% lack governance frameworks. 21% don't use explainability methods. No binding standard yet but signals future regulation.
- **Cloud Computing (JC2)**: Requires cloud architecture, governance, vendor risk management, data sovereignty skills. Guidance now; binding standard in development.
- **Open Finance**: Requires API development, consent management, digital identity skills. Phased: voluntary then mandatory.
- **Basel III**: Requires quantitative risk modelling, data engineering, regulatory reporting upgrades. 43% of unfilled banking positions due to lack of relevant education.
- **POPIA Amendments**: Requires consent management tech, DSAR handling across channels (including WhatsApp), privacy management. Effective immediately April 2025.
- **PA Strategy 2025-2030**: Signals AI governance, cloud, cyber, crypto as strategic focus areas.

Additional findings:
- **King V Code** (effective Jan 2026) makes data/technology/AI a standalone governance pillar — boards must be "technologically literate."
- **BankSETA**: 43% of unfilled positions due to skills/education gaps. Cybersecurity flagged as most critical gap. Declining maths/science pass rates reduce pipeline.
- **IOBSA**: Identifies 5 key future banker skills — data analytics, digital platform management, cybersecurity, CX design, RegTech.
- **Absa Cybersecurity Academy**: 3-year programme with 12 international certifications; new cohort July 2024.
- **UWC**: Launched SA's first PG Diploma in Fintech Law and Regulation (Oct 2025).
- Cross-cutting skills demand matrix shows **data governance** is required by almost all regulatory changes, followed by **board digital literacy** and **cybersecurity**.

**Decision**: The research is comprehensive and provides strong material for the Analyst. Proceed to Sub-task 3 — dispatch the Analyst to evaluate and rank the regulatory changes by significance to digital skills requirements, selecting and justifying the top three.

---

## Step 3: Significance Assessment
**Agent type**: Analyst
**Task assigned**: Evaluate each of the 8 identified regulatory changes against three criteria — (a) breadth of impact across the sector, (b) urgency of compliance timeline, (c) scale of the new digital skills gap created — using a 1-5 scoring scale. Select and justify the top three most significant changes. Note close calls and caveats.
**Key findings**: The Analyst produced a rigorous scored evaluation:

| Rank | Regulatory Change | Breadth | Urgency | Skills Gap | Composite |
|------|-------------------|---------|---------|------------|-----------|
| 1 | Joint Standard 2 of 2024 — Cybersecurity & Cyber Resilience | 5 | 5 | 5 | **15** |
| 2 | Joint Standard 1 of 2023 — IT Governance & Risk Management | 5 | 5 | 3 | **13** |
| 3 | Basel III Post-Crisis Reforms | 3 | 5 | 4 | **12** |
| 4 | Amended POPIA Regulations | 4 | 5 | 2 | 11 |
| 5 | Cloud Computing Guidance | 4 | 2 | 3 | 9 |
| 6 | AI Report | 3 | 1 | 4 | 8 |
| 7 | Open Finance Recommendations | 3 | 1 | 3 | 7 |
| 8 | King V Code | 3 | 2 | 2 | 7 |

**Top three justifications:**
1. **Cybersecurity (JS2)**: Perfect 15/15 — universal sector scope, binding and in force, mandates annual training at all levels including board, and faces the most severe skills gap (63% of cyber roles unfilled, 35,000 national vacancies).
2. **IT Governance (JS1)**: 13/15 — same universal scope and binding status, but skills gap is incremental (established disciplines like COBIT/ITIL/ISO 27001) rather than novel. Foundational — failure here undermines compliance with JS2, cloud, and AI standards.
3. **Basel III**: 12/15 — narrower scope (banks only, specialist roles) but maximum urgency and deep specialist skills gap. Quantitative modelling, data engineering, and model validation skills are structurally scarce due to declining maths/science pass rates.

**Close calls**: POPIA amendments (11/15) narrowly missed — high urgency but incremental skills demands. AI report has highest future potential but zero current binding force.

**Decision**: The analysis is thorough and well-reasoned. Proceed to Sub-task 4 — dispatch the Synthesiser to produce the final report.

---

## Step 4: Final Report Synthesis
**Agent type**: Synthesiser
**Task assigned**: Produce a polished, well-structured research report answering the original question. Draw on all findings from Steps 1-3. Include an executive summary, methodology note, detailed section for each of the top three regulatory changes, cross-cutting analysis, limitations, and sources. Target audience: professional readers familiar with banking and regulation but not necessarily SA regulatory specialists.
**Key findings**: The Synthesiser produced a complete report (~4,000 words) structured as follows:
- **Executive summary** framing the convergence of regulatory intensity and skills scarcity
- **Methodology** explaining the three-criteria scoring framework
- **Three detailed sections** covering JS2 (Cybersecurity), JS1 (IT Governance), and Basel III, each with subsections on requirements, skills demanded, skills gap evidence, and compliance timeline
- **Cross-cutting analysis** addressing layered demands, King V governance implications, the structural pipeline problem (declining STEM pass rates), and near-miss regulatory changes (POPIA, AI)
- **Limitations** acknowledging data availability, methodological variation, ranking sensitivity, regulatory pipeline, and sector focus
- **Sources** organised by category
**Decision**: Report is complete and meets the brief. Write final outputs to `report.md` and `process_log.md`.
