# Skills shortages reshaping South Africa's banking workforce

South Africa's banking sector faces a convergence of skills shortages that will intensify through 2029, driven by simultaneous regulatory overhaul, accelerating digital transformation, and a global war for specialist talent. **The most critical gaps are in AI and data science, cybersecurity, advanced regulatory compliance, cloud computing, and ESG-related competencies** — areas where demand is surging but the domestic talent pipeline remains structurally underdeveloped. For an organisation developing training programmes, the clearest market opportunity lies in mid-career professional upskilling at NQF 7–8 level, where a pronounced gap exists between entry-level BANKSETA learnerships and generic executive education offered by business schools. No structured, sector-wide programme currently addresses this segment with banking-specific content in digital transformation, regulatory mastery, and emerging competencies.

The stakes are high. SA banks collectively spent over **R40 billion on IT in 2024** (excluding staff costs), with Capitec's technology budget growing 32% year-on-year and Absa's rising 13%. Yet 84% of large South African corporations report difficulty filling highly skilled positions, ICT specialists now represent 22% of all critical skills shortages nationally (up from 10% two years ago), and an estimated **30,000 cybersecurity professionals** are needed to close the sector's security talent deficit. Banks are increasingly building internal academies — Absa's "grow our own timber" strategy, Capitec's 10-month internal tech reskilling learnership, Standard Bank's Code Crowd programme — but these proprietary efforts cannot address the sector-wide scale of the challenge.

## Five forces are creating unprecedented demand for new banking capabilities

The skills crisis is not a single problem but a compound effect of five converging forces, each generating distinct competency demands that overlap and reinforce each other.

**Digital transformation is the dominant driver.** Every major SA bank identifies digital transformation as a core strategic priority. Standard Bank, the largest IT spender at R12.7 billion, is modernising core banking systems, CRM platforms, and AI-enabled contact centres. Nedbank completed its multi-year "Managed Evolution" programme in 2024. Capitec's cloud costs rose 39% as it migrates critical infrastructure to AWS. FNB has built a "super-app" integrating lifestyle services beyond banking, and **89% of South Africans now exclusively use digital banking channels**. This transformation demands skills in cloud architecture, API development, DevOps, microservices, and modern programming languages — capabilities that legacy mainframe-trained staff do not possess.

**AI adoption has crossed a critical threshold.** A joint SARB/FSCA report published in November 2025 found that over **50% of SA banking institutions have adopted AI**, with more than half planning to invest over R20 million further. Banks are deploying AI across credit scoring, fraud detection, customer service chatbots, and document analysis. McKinsey projects generative and agentic AI will deliver $200–$340 billion in annual banking productivity impact globally. Accenture estimates AI can boost banking productivity by 22–30%. The skills implications extend well beyond data science teams: credit analysts must work with AI-augmented decision-making, compliance officers need to understand AI governance, and relationship managers must interpret AI-powered customer insights.

**Regulatory complexity is escalating sharply.** The 2026–2029 period brings an extraordinary concentration of regulatory change. Basel III.1 output floors are phasing in from 60% (July 2025) to 72.5% (January 2028), with the Fundamental Review of the Trading Book requiring full implementation from January 2026. The COFI Bill received Cabinet approval for parliamentary submission in March 2026, with a three-year transition period that will demand wholesale retraining in outcomes-based regulation. JIBAR ceases publication after December 2026, requiring transition to ZARONIA. Although South Africa exited the FATF grey list in October 2025, the Fifth Round Mutual Evaluation beginning in 2026 means compliance teams cannot ease up. And a new Joint Standard on Cloud Computing and Data Offshoring is under development, adding technology governance requirements to the compliance stack.

**Cybersecurity threats are outpacing defensive capability.** South Africa experienced a 1,200% increase in deepfake-related attacks in a single year. An estimated 63% of cybersecurity roles in the country remain partially or completely unfilled. Only 14% of global banking leaders say they have adequate cybersecurity talent. Brain drain — emigration of IT professionals to better-paying overseas markets — has particularly hollowed out SA's security workforce. The Cybercrimes Act, POPIA enforcement, and SARB's cybersecurity and cyber-resilience directives all demand specialist capabilities that are acutely scarce.

**ESG and climate risk requirements are emerging fast.** The FSCA has signalled its intention to introduce mandatory climate-related disclosures aligned with IFRS S2, taking a "climate-first approach." The Prudential Authority's Guidance Note 3 of 2025 provides detailed voluntary climate disclosure guidance aligned with IFRS S1/S2, with the explicit expectation that these will become mandatory. Nedbank is actively recruiting climate risk specialists and sustainable finance experts. Investec has upskilled its entire board in climate-related matters. Yet an RBSA survey found **59% of SA's largest financial institutions had never discussed climate-related financial risks at board level** — indicating the gap between where the sector is and where it needs to be.

## The seven most critical skills gaps for mid-career banking professionals

Drawing on BANKSETA's Sector Skills Plan 2025–2030, major bank integrated reports, SARB/FSCA publications, and international consulting firm analyses, seven thematic skills gaps emerge as most urgent for mid-career upskilling programmes.

### AI, data science, and advanced analytics

This is the single largest skills gap across the sector. Every major bank identifies data science and AI as priority recruitment and development areas. Capitec reports that **15% of all external appointments in 2024 were data and technology experts**. Absa directs the majority of its 13% IT spending growth toward data analytics. Nedbank's Quants programme specifically seeks skills in R, Python, SQL, MATLAB, and SAS. The WEF ranks AI and big data as the fastest-growing skill globally through 2030. Mid-career professionals need capabilities spanning machine learning fundamentals, prompt engineering for generative AI, AI governance and ethics, data literacy for non-technical staff, and MLOps for model deployment. Crucially, the SARB/FSCA AI report recommends that banks invest specifically in prompt engineering training and AI-related employee development.

### Cybersecurity and cyber resilience

With 30,000 unfilled positions nationally and 63% of roles vacant, cybersecurity represents the most acute supply-demand imbalance. The BANKSETA and SABRIC have partnered to develop a National Cybersecurity Skills Framework, and Absa runs a Cybersecurity Academy training 300 graduates per year. However, no comprehensive NQF-accredited banking-specific cybersecurity programme exists — professionals rely entirely on international certifications (CISSP, CISM, CISA) without SA banking contextualisation. Mid-career needs span security operations, incident response, cloud security architecture, DevSecOps, cyber GRC, and digital forensics. The intersection of cybersecurity with POPIA compliance and the Cybercrimes Act creates uniquely South African requirements.

### Regulatory and compliance specialisation

The simultaneous implementation of Basel III.1, the COFI Bill, enhanced AML/CFT requirements, cloud governance standards, POPIA amendments, and crypto asset regulation creates compound compliance demands that existing staff are not equipped for. The shift from rules-based to outcomes-based regulation under COFI requires fundamentally different compliance thinking — the FSCA has stated this is "one of the most challenging aspects" of the transition. Basel III.1 demands advanced credit risk modelling, FRTB expertise, and operational risk measurement capabilities. The JIBAR-to-ZARONIA transition requires treasury and ALM teams to retool entirely. No comprehensive programme currently covers the full SA regulatory landscape together — training tends to be fragmented across individual regulations.

### Cloud computing and infrastructure modernisation

All major banks are actively migrating to cloud, with Absa operating as "one of the few major African banks as a cloud-native entity" through its AWS strategic partnership. Standard Bank partners with Microsoft Azure, AWS, and Salesforce. The PA's forthcoming Joint Standard on Cloud Computing and Data Offshoring will impose new governance requirements. Skills needed include cloud architecture (AWS/Azure certifications), DevOps engineering, infrastructure-as-code, FinOps (cloud cost optimisation), site reliability engineering, and cloud security specialisation. Nedbank has launched a specific Cloud Learnership covering cloud computing, DevOps, FinOps, cloud architecture, and site reliability engineering — signalling how seriously banks view this gap. The most affected mid-career employees are on-premises infrastructure engineers, network engineers, and system administrators.

### ESG, climate risk, and sustainable finance

This is an emerging but rapidly growing gap. The PA's guidance requires banks to report across four pillars — governance, strategy, risk management, and metrics/targets — aligned with BCBS and ISSB frameworks. Skills needed include climate scenario analysis and stress testing using NGFS scenarios, green finance taxonomy application, Scope 1/2/3 emissions accounting, sustainable finance product structuring (green bonds, sustainability-linked loans), and just transition risk assessment. South Africa's dependence on fossil fuels makes the just transition dimension particularly important. The National Treasury published the Green Finance Taxonomy (1st Edition) in 2022, but only 43% of SA companies plan to implement ISSB standards, and 36% have not even decided — indicating significant latent demand for capability building.

### Leadership and management in transformation

BASA's Transformation Report reveals that only **36% of top senior managers are Black** against a 60% target, and the gap at middle management is similarly persistent at 68% versus 70%. Simultaneously, Deloitte identifies middle manager roles as "ripe for reinvention" in the AI era, and McKinsey calls for "G3" leadership models (CEO + CFO + CHRO working together). Absa's Leadership Academy served 26,759 colleagues in 2024, with 72% female participants. The BANKSETA SSP 2025–2030 identifies Management and Leadership Development as one of five strategic priorities, driven by the "escalating war for talent" and equity transformation requirements. Mid-career banking professionals need skills in leading hybrid teams, managing human-AI collaboration, change management for digital transformation, and the strategic judgement to navigate simultaneous disruption across technology, regulation, and customer expectations.

### Open banking, API ecosystems, and platform strategy

SARB's Payments Ecosystem Modernisation programme has opened the National Payment System to fintechs for the first time. PayShap, launched in 2023, processes nearly 1 million transactions monthly as SA's first cloud-based instant payment service. The FSCA's Draft Position Paper on Open Finance signals phased regulatory rollout. Banks need staff who understand API design and management, platform business strategy, developer experience, digital identity and consent management, and third-party integration governance. Product managers, payment operations staff, and business development professionals are most affected — they need to shift from product thinking to platform thinking.

## What major banks are doing — and where they're falling short

South Africa's six major banks are responding with varying strategies, but common patterns and gaps emerge.

**Internal reskilling is accelerating.** Capitec's innovative 10-month internal tech reskilling learnership allows non-technical employees to pivot permanently to the technology division at full salary — a model other banks have not yet matched. Standard Bank runs Power User, Code Crowd, and Global Elite programmes targeting automation and cloud skills. Absa's Udemy for Business platform logged **141,149 learning hours** across 29,794 active users in 2024, with 54.4% of consumption in technology skills. These programmes demonstrate banks' commitment to "build versus buy" talent strategies.

**Graduate pipelines dominate investment.** All six banks operate extensive graduate, learnership, and bursary programmes. Nedbank enrolls over 200 graduates annually across quantitative analytics, IT, risk, and compliance streams. FNB's graduate programme has placed 250+ graduates annually since 2006. However, **these programmes overwhelmingly target entry-level talent, not mid-career professionals**. The sector lacks equivalent structured development pathways for experienced employees who need to reskill or specialise.

**The education-industry gap is widening.** Investec's former Group CIO Malcolm Laing articulated this plainly: "University curriculums are not keeping up with the pace of technological change... universities need a stable curriculum, while the tech industry is changing its methods and means every 18 months." This disconnect has prompted banks to build proprietary academies, but these are siloed and non-transferable across the sector.

**IT spending reveals strategic priorities.** Standard Bank leads at R12.7 billion, followed by FirstRand at R10.9 billion, Nedbank at R7.4 billion, and Absa at R6.8 billion. Capitec's 32% year-on-year growth rate — the highest among peers — reflects its digital-native DNA. Absa's decision to impair R2.4 billion in software (March 2026) after a strategy rethink illustrates the high stakes and frequent pivots in technology transformation.

## The training landscape has a structural gap at mid-career level

The existing training ecosystem for SA banking professionals reveals a pronounced missing middle.

**BANKSETA's programmes are strong on entry-level but weak on mid-career.** The authority collected R1.146 billion in skills development levies in 2024/25, achieving 84% overall performance against targets. Learnership completions nearly doubled targets at 1,027. However, learnerships operate at NQF Level 4–6, well below what experienced professionals require (NQF 7–8+). BANKSETA's discretionary grant spending actually declined 20% to R679 million, and R520 million in critical skills development budgets went unspent — suggesting capacity constraints in deploying funding effectively at higher levels. BANKSETA's five strategic priorities for 2025–2030 (technology/digitalisation, compliance/risk, management/leadership, national strategy alignment, and markets/products) are well-aligned with the gaps identified, but execution through higher-level programmes is underdeveloped.

**Universities offer quality but limited banking specificity.** UCT's MPhil in Financial Technology is the only dedicated fintech master's in Africa but is highly selective. GIBS and WBS provide excellent executive education without banking specialisation. Milpark is the only institution offering a full suite of banking-specific qualifications (up to NQF 8 Postgraduate Diploma in Banking), making it the closest existing provider to the mid-career need — but its programmes do not yet cover AI, cybersecurity, or ESG in depth.

**Professional bodies accredit but don't deliver.** IOBSA provides the Chartered Banker designation (NQF 8) but does not directly deliver training. SAIFM offers targeted financial markets workshops. The Compliance Institute and IRMSA provide designations and CPD but lack comprehensive banking-specific programmes. CFA and FRM remain popular international certifications but address investment and risk rather than the emerging skills identified here. No SA-specific banking technology certification exists — professionals rely entirely on international vendor certifications with no local contextualisation.

**The gap is clear.** There is no structured, sector-wide programme for mid-career banking professionals (5–15 years experience) that integrates digital transformation, regulatory mastery, leadership development, and emerging banking competencies in a flexible delivery format suited to working professionals. This is the single largest opportunity for a training organisation serving the sector.

## Recommended training priorities for programme development

Based on the convergence of regulatory drivers, technology transformation pressures, bank strategies, industry body priorities, and international trends, the following training priorities should guide programme development, ranked by urgency and market demand.

**Tier 1 — Immediate and critical (launch 2026–2027):**

The first priority should be an **AI and data literacy programme for banking professionals** — not a data science degree, but practical capability in working with AI tools, understanding ML model outputs, prompt engineering, data-driven decision-making, and AI governance within SA's regulatory context. This addresses the single most cited skills gap across every source, and the SARB/FSCA's explicit recommendation for prompt engineering and AI training. Content should range from data literacy for non-technical managers to applied ML for analysts.

The second immediate priority is **regulatory and compliance transformation**, covering Basel III.1 implementation (FRTB, output floors, operational risk), the COFI Bill transition to outcomes-based regulation, enhanced AML/CFT for the FATF Fifth Round Mutual Evaluation, POPIA amendments, and the ZARONIA transition. No existing programme covers this regulatory convergence comprehensively. Content should be modular so professionals can focus on their specific regulatory domain while understanding the interconnections.

Third, **cybersecurity for banking** should combine international certification preparation (CISSP, CISM pathways) with SA-specific content — POPIA compliance, the Cybercrimes Act, SARB cyber resilience directives, and threat landscape analysis specific to the South African financial sector.

**Tier 2 — High priority (launch 2027–2028):**

A **cloud computing and digital infrastructure programme** covering cloud architecture, DevOps, cloud security, FinOps, and the PA's emerging cloud governance requirements would address the skills gap created by sector-wide cloud migration. Content should be practical and certification-aligned (AWS/Azure) with SA banking regulatory overlay.

An **ESG, climate risk, and sustainable finance programme** should cover the PA's four-pillar climate disclosure framework, green finance taxonomy application, climate scenario analysis, sustainable finance product structuring, and just transition considerations. First-mover advantage exists here as mandatory disclosure approaches.

**Tier 3 — Important and developing (launch 2028–2029):**

A **banking leadership for transformation programme** at NQF 8 level should address managing human-AI collaboration, leading hybrid teams, change management for digital transformation, equity transformation at senior levels, and strategic judgement in complex environments. This directly addresses BANKSETA's management and leadership priority and BASA's transformation targets.

An **open banking, API ecosystems, and platform strategy programme** should cover API design, platform business models, third-party integration governance, digital identity management, and the evolving SA open finance regulatory framework.

**Cross-cutting design principles:** All programmes should adopt modular, stackable micro-credential formats suitable for working professionals. Content should be banking-contextualised rather than generic. Where possible, programmes should align with BANKSETA discretionary grant eligibility to leverage the R1.1 billion+ annual levy pool. NQF 7–8 level positioning addresses the structural gap between entry-level learnerships and generic executive education. Partnership with IOBSA for CPD accreditation would enhance credibility. A blended delivery model — combining online self-paced learning with periodic face-to-face workshops — matches the needs of mid-career professionals who cannot take extended leave from demanding banking roles.

## Conclusion

South Africa's banking sector is entering a three-year period of extraordinary skills disruption. The compound effect of Basel III.1 implementation, COFI Bill transition, AI adoption at scale, cloud migration, enhanced cybersecurity demands, and approaching mandatory ESG disclosure creates a skills challenge that no single bank or existing programme can address alone. The BANKSETA's own data reveals the paradox: 65% of the banking workforce lacks post-matric qualifications, yet the sector's most urgent needs are at NQF 7–8+ level in highly technical domains.

The most consequential insight for training programme design is that **the mid-career professional segment is dramatically underserved**. Banks are investing billions in technology but training budgets overwhelmingly target graduates. International consulting firms uniformly recommend reskilling over recruiting — McKinsey finds 75% of banking executives agree reskilling is critical, and Accenture notes 73% of banks still need to reconfigure existing roles. The organisation that builds modular, banking-specific, regulator-aligned training at the right NQF level for experienced professionals will address both a commercial opportunity and a structural weakness that the sector's own leaders have identified but not yet solved. The window is now: COFI's three-year transition starts in 2026, Basel III.1 output floors tighten annually through 2028, and the SARB/FSCA expect AI governance frameworks to be in place imminently. Banks need their people ready — and current training infrastructure is not delivering at the pace or level required.
