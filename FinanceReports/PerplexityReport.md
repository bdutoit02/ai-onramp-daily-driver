# Skills Shortages in the South African Banking Sector: Training Priorities 2026–2028

**Prepared for:** [Training Organisation Name]
**Purpose:** Strategic guidance for programme development
**Date:** April 2026

***

## Executive Summary

The South African banking sector is undergoing profound structural transformation, driven simultaneously by aggressive digital adoption, an expanding regulatory perimeter, the global race for AI capability, and persistent talent attrition through emigration. These converging forces are widening skills gaps across the sector faster than conventional education and training pipelines can close them — creating a genuine strategic opening for a focused, well-positioned training provider.

The finance sector remains one of South Africa's most dynamic employment segments, having added 460,000 jobs to the economy in 2023 and generating combined headline earnings of R113.2 billion — a 13.8% increase year-on-year. Yet 47.73% of all re-advertised job vacancies in South Africa come from the finance sector, a telling signal of how difficult it is to fill roles with appropriately skilled candidates. Industry analysis confirms the problem: 58% of South African business respondents cite talent gaps as a major obstacle to growth, a rate that exceeds even economic headwinds as the primary constraint.[^1][^2][^3]

This report identifies six priority skills shortage clusters that will define workforce pressures in South African banking over the next three years, and translates each into actionable training priorities for programme development.

***

## Sector Context: Why Skills Gaps Are Widening

### The Structural Drivers

The BANKSETA 2025–2030 Sector Skills Plan identifies five major change drivers reshaping skills demand in the banking and alternative banking sector: digitalisation and new technologies; changing customer expectations; regulatory changes, risk and cybercrime; economic shifts and geopolitics; and ESG requirements. These drivers do not operate in isolation — they interact and compound. The shift toward AI-driven banking, for example, simultaneously creates demand for data scientists and machine learning engineers while generating new compliance obligations around algorithmic transparency and model risk management.[^2]

South Africa's major banks — Standard Bank, FNB/FirstRand, Absa, and Nedbank — are entering 2026 in a new phase of their AI journey, moving from pilot projects to large-scale deployment across core operations. Approximately 45% of South African banks have each allocated more than R30 million for AI investments in 2026, with a strong focus on autonomous agents, predictive security, and fraud detection. This industrial-scale AI deployment will require a substantially upskilled workforce that does not yet exist in sufficient numbers.[^4]

### The Brain Drain Constraint

The skills challenge is compounded by a persistent and worsening brain drain. More than one million South Africans now live abroad, and 43% say they do not plan to return permanently. Between 2017 and 2024, over 51,500 taxpayers formally ended their South African tax residency. In the ICT and banking domains, the departure of IT professionals has fuelled skyrocketing salary expectations and left financial institutions increasingly vulnerable to cyberattacks. A 2024 study by Nelson Mandela University's STEP Research Group found a significant shortage of data scientists, system engineers, and programmers across South Africa, a gap that is widened further by competition from financial institutions in the UK, Europe, and the Gulf, which actively recruit South African professionals.[^5][^6][^7]

In response, 80% of South Africa's leading employers now manage talent pipelines through educational partnerships and professional development programmes — signalling a structural shift from reactive recruitment toward proactive skills-building. This creates a strong market for training providers that can partner with banks to develop internal capability.[^7]

## Priority Skills Shortage Clusters

### 1. Data Science, Analytics, and AI Engineering

**Nature of the shortage:** This is the most acute and widely documented shortage in the sector. The BANKSETA SPOI priority occupations list ranks Data Management Manager (sector need: 489), Software Developer (sector need: 237), ICT Systems Analyst (sector need: 147), Developer Programmer (sector need: 143), and Data Scientist (sector need: 55) among its top ten priority occupations for 2025–2030. Data Management Manager has featured in the hard-to-fill vacancies (HTFV) list with 51 vacancies, consistently appearing across the sector's size tiers.[^2]

The shortage is structural, not cyclical. South African universities produce insufficient graduates with the combination of financial domain knowledge and technical data expertise that banks require. Institutions of higher learning have been criticised for inadequately equipping graduates with the contextual banking awareness needed to apply technical skills effectively in financial environments. This "double gap" — technical capability without domain context, or banking expertise without technical fluency — makes it extremely difficult for banks to hire directly from standard graduate pipelines.[^8]

**AI-specific dimension:** The FSCA and Prudential Authority's inaugural joint report on artificial intelligence in the financial sector (published November 2025) confirms that banking and payments institutions lead AI adoption, with over half already deploying AI solutions. FNB's AI-powered fraud and risk systems alone saved the bank and its customers over R1.1 billion in a single year and freed up 70% of analysts' time. Yet 38% of banks report that the shortage of AI skills directly slows their deployment ambitions. The sector urgently needs practitioners who can build and operate machine learning pipelines, develop explainable AI models, manage model risk, and govern AI systems in alignment with the Treating Customers Fairly (TCF) framework.[^9][^10][^11]

**Training priority:** Applied data analytics for bankers, machine learning fundamentals, AI governance and model risk management, data engineering, Python for financial analysis, and bank-contextualised data science bootcamps. Short-to-medium programme formats (weeks to three months) targeting in-service employees will be most effective, given the urgency and the impracticality of waiting for four-year degree pipelines.

### 2. Cybersecurity and Digital Risk Management

**Nature of the shortage:** South Africa faces a national cybersecurity crisis that falls disproportionately on the financial sector. A 2024 CSIR national survey found that 63% of cybersecurity roles in South Africa remain partially or fully unfilled. The country recorded 17,849 ransomware detections in 2024 — the highest on the African continent. Nearly 40% of South African organisations reported losses exceeding one million US dollars from cyberattacks in the past year.[^12][^13][^14]

Financial institutions are bearing a disproportionate share of this exposure. The 2024 African Financial Industry Barometer (produced by AFIS and Deloitte) found that 59% of institutions identify cybercrime as a major threat. In the EMEA region, 51% of organisations attributed security breaches to a lack of cybersecurity skills and training. The Fortinet 2024 Cybersecurity Skills Gap Global Research Report shows that 70% of global respondents believe the cybersecurity skills shortage creates additional risks for their organisations. More alarmingly, 58% of security leaders attribute serious breaches to human error arising from a lack of basic security awareness — an issue that training can directly address.[^6][^5]

The brain drain is especially damaging in this domain. South Africa and Nigeria have experienced significant emigration of IT security professionals, driving salary expectations upward and leaving financial institutions with a combination of vacancies and underqualified personnel. Demand for cybersecurity analysts and incident response specialists is significantly outpacing supply.[^6]

**Training priority:** Role-specific cybersecurity awareness programmes for all bank staff (not just IT teams), specialist training in financial sector threat intelligence and incident response, anti-money laundering systems security, cloud security fundamentals, and training on the POPIA compliance requirements tied to data breach management. Programmes aligned to the SARB's cybersecurity and cyber-resilience Joint Standard will carry particular credibility with compliance teams.[^10]

### 3. Risk, Compliance, and Regulatory Management

**Nature of the shortage:** South Africa's regulatory environment has intensified dramatically, creating surging demand for skilled compliance and risk professionals. Several simultaneous regulatory shifts are driving this demand.

**Basel IV** came into effect in South Africa from 1 July 2025, introducing revised standardised approaches for credit risk, revised internal ratings-based approaches, a revised operational risk framework, and output floor requirements that phase up to 72.5% by January 2028. Banks require staff with the technical expertise to implement and maintain these new capital models — skills that are in short supply globally, not just locally.[^15]

**FATF greylisting** (February 2023) requires South Africa to demonstrate a sustained increase in the effective identification, investigation, and prosecution of money laundering and terrorist financing activities. Banks have been explicitly required to conduct training and awareness programmes on AML/CFT obligations, particularly regarding the filing of Suspicious Transaction Reports. The FSCA's 2025–2028 Regulatory Strategy confirms that supervisory enforcement is intensifying, with R943 million in administrative penalties imposed on 31 individuals in 2023/24 alone.[^16][^17]

**The COFI Bill**, while still pending promulgation, will eventually replace all existing market conduct legislation with a single, outcomes-based regulatory framework. The FSCA has confirmed that COFI "is the reality of how the financial sector in South Africa will be regulated" and remains a top regulatory priority. Its implementation will require widespread training on new conduct obligations, licensing requirements, and fair treatment of customer (TCF) standards.[^18][^19]

**Organisational Risk Manager** is among the occupations appearing most frequently on the BANKSETA HTFV trend lists over five years, and Compliance Officer features prominently in the skills gaps by occupation analysis.[^1][^2]

**Training priority:** AML/CFT compliance for financial institutions (aligned to FATF requirements and the FIC Act), Basel IV capital management, credit risk modelling, operational risk frameworks, conduct risk under the COFI Bill, TCF implementation, and senior manager accountability under South Africa's emerging accountability frameworks. Compliance-focused short courses with Continuing Professional Development (CPD) accreditation will attract practitioners seeking to meet regulatory fit-and-proper requirements.

### 4. AI Governance, Responsible AI, and Model Risk Management

**Nature of the shortage:** This is a newly emergent but rapidly growing shortage that sits at the intersection of technology, ethics, law, and risk management. The FSCA/PA November 2025 AI report identified several key lessons for the sector: financial institutions need comprehensive governance structures including strong data governance, model risk management, and board-level oversight; they need clear disclosure frameworks for AI-driven decisions affecting customers (such as credit scoring); and they must ensure AI deployment aligns with the TCF framework.[^20][^11]

South Africa's national AI Policy (moving toward approval in 2026) establishes five core pillars, of which skills capacity and responsible governance are explicit priorities. The policy favours a sector-specific, multi-regulator model, meaning AI governance will be embedded within the PA and FSCA's existing supervisory frameworks — making bank-specific AI governance competency a regulatory necessity, not merely a best practice.[^21]

The challenge is that most available AI training focuses on technical implementation rather than governance, ethics, and regulatory alignment. The FSCA's expectation that financial institutions develop accountable persons with responsibility for AI and ML frameworks, implement board-level oversight, and maintain ethical guidelines creates a specific demand for governance-oriented training that combines regulatory literacy with practical AI risk management.[^22]

**Training priority:** AI governance frameworks for financial institutions, model risk management (aligned to PA guidance), explainable AI for regulated environments, AI ethics and bias detection, board-level AI literacy for executives and non-executive directors, and POPIA compliance in AI decision-making. This is an underprovided but high-value training niche with strong demand from both risk teams and executive leadership.

### 5. Credit, Financial Analysis, and Quantitative Skills

**Nature of the shortage:** Credit and loans officers represent the second highest hard-to-fill vacancy in the sector (64 vacancies), while Credit Manager has appeared in the top ten HTFV list five times over the five-year trend period — more frequently than almost any other role. Finance Manager features in both the SPOI priority occupations list and the skills gap analysis. Quantitative/Mathematical Analyst (NQF Level 9 bursary) is explicitly prioritised as a critical scarce skill.[^2]

The high non-performing loan environment — NPLs increased by 21% in 2023 — has intensified pressure on banks to strengthen their credit risk management capabilities. Banks are moving from reactive to proactive credit management, using AI-enabled early warning systems and portfolio analytics, which require analysts who combine traditional credit assessment skills with the ability to work with algorithmic outputs and data models.[^4][^2]

Competition for quantitative analysts in South Africa's major financial centres (Johannesburg, Cape Town) is exceptionally fierce. Nedbank, ABSA, Standard Bank, and others run dedicated graduate quantitative programmes precisely because the open market cannot supply sufficient candidates. The research skill — the ability to conduct rigorous financial analysis — is similarly highlighted as a significant gap across the sector.[^23][^1]

**Training priority:** Credit risk analysis (incorporating AI-assisted tools), financial modelling, quantitative analysis fundamentals, Basel IV credit risk approaches, non-performing loan management strategies, and applied statistics for risk management. Programmes designed for intermediate-level practitioners seeking to upgrade credit skills in the context of the new regulatory capital framework will be particularly well-timed.

### 6. Management, Leadership, and Transformation Skills

**Nature of the shortage:** The leadership and management skills gap in South African banking has both a technical and a transformation dimension. Corporate General Manager carries the largest sector need of any occupation in the SPOI list (554), and Business Training Manager features prominently in the HTFVs. EY research finds that only approximately 44% of banking executives believe their organisations have the digital skills and mindset needed for transformation, and only around four in ten report that their organisational cultures have a genuine sense of urgency about change and innovation.[^24][^2]

The B-BBEE transformation dimension adds further complexity. The BASA Transformation Report 2024 shows that while banks are ahead of amended Financial Sector Charter targets — with 48% of bank directors now black South Africans and R5.648 billion spent on skills development in 2023 — a meaningful gap persists at senior management level. Research confirms that leaders tasked with implementing B-BBEE require specific competencies: emotional intelligence, social and cultural intelligence, inclusive leadership capabilities, and group facilitation skills. These are not naturally developed through traditional business education programmes.[^25][^26]

The COFI Bill will impose new fit-and-proper requirements on board members and senior managers of financial institutions. The FSCA and PA have both called on boards and executives to take personal responsibility for regulatory compliance, citing instances where sanctions have been levied against individuals for governance failures. This regulatory accountability pressure is creating specific demand for governance-focused leadership training.[^16][^18]

**Training priority:** Digital leadership and change management for bank executives, transformation leadership (B-BBEE implementation, inclusive leadership), governance for bank boards and audit committees (aligned to the PA and FSCA accountability frameworks), strategic workforce planning, and people-centric transformation management. Programmes targeting middle management who are being developed for senior roles will align well with BASA's stated intention to build a pipeline of black senior managers.[^26]

## Emerging Skill Areas to Monitor

BANKSETA's 2025–2030 SSP identifies five emerging occupations that have not yet achieved formal OFO classification but are growing rapidly in sector demand:[^2]

- **AI and Machine Learning Specialist** — driven by the industrial-scale AI deployment described above
- **Blockchain and Data Engineer** — linked to digital assets, cross-border payments infrastructure, and tokenisation
- **Sustainability/ESG Specialist** — driven by NetZero commitments and sustainable finance obligations; Standard Bank launched a dedicated digital Sustainability Academy in partnership with Microsoft in 2025[^27]
- **Cybersecurity Personnel** — see Priority Cluster 2
- **Mixed Reality Designer** — early-stage, but relevant to customer experience innovation

These roles represent investment opportunities for forward-looking programme development over a two-to-four year horizon.

## Strategic Recommendations for Programme Development

### Position at the Intersection of Domain Knowledge and Technical Skills

The most critical and underserved gap in the South African banking market is not pure technology training (widely available) or pure banking knowledge (well established), but programmes that combine both. Banks consistently report that graduates entering data, AI, and risk roles lack contextual banking knowledge, while experienced bankers lack the technical literacy to work effectively alongside AI systems or interpret quantitative model outputs. Training programmes that teach data science *in the context of banking*, or that teach AI governance *from a regulatory perspective*, will command a premium and are difficult for generic tech providers or universities to replicate quickly.[^8][^1]

### Prioritise Short-to-Medium Formats with CPD Recognition

Given the pace of regulatory and technological change, bank employees and their employers strongly favour modular, stackable qualifications over long programmes. Compliance-focused content is particularly suited to short CPD-accredited modules, given the "fit and proper" requirements associated with regulatory roles and the need for annual refresher training. Programmes aligned to BANKSETA PIVOTAL funding criteria will also be able to attract discretionary grant support for employer partners.[^28]

### Target BANKSETA Funding Alignment

BANKSETA's 2025–2030 SPOI list provides a ready-made funding roadmap. Programmes mapped to the top priority occupations — Data Management Manager, Software Developer, ICT Systems Analyst, Developer Programmer, and Data Scientist — are eligible for BANKSETA discretionary grants and will attract employer co-funding. The SETA Finance Cluster partnership (BANKSETA, MICTSETA, InSETA, FASSET) is also exploring joint programmes in common scarce skills areas, opening potential for cross-sectoral programme delivery.[^2]

### Develop AI Governance as a Standalone Niche

The FSCA/PA AI report and South Africa's emerging national AI policy framework have created a specific, time-bound training opportunity in AI governance for financial institutions. This is currently underserved by the existing training market, which skews heavily toward technical AI skills. Programmes targeting risk officers, compliance teams, board members, and senior managers — focused on governance frameworks, model risk management, ethical AI deployment, and regulatory alignment — address a gap that will only grow as the PA and FSCA tighten oversight.[^20][^22]

### Build SME and Alternative Banking Delivery Capacity

BANKSETA data shows that small enterprises face the most acute per-capita skills gaps, with 626 hard-to-fill vacancies compared to 682 for large firms. However, large firms have established internal training academies and direct SETA relationships. The greatest *relative* opportunity for a training provider may lie in the alternative banking subsector — microfinance institutions, cooperative banks, and challenger banks — which have the same technical and regulatory skill needs as major banks but far less internal capacity to address them. Accessible, affordable, online-first programmes are most likely to penetrate this segment.[^2]

### Address the Brain Drain Through Employer Retention Partnerships

Given that talent flight is a primary structural constraint on skills supply, training providers that help banks retain staff — by demonstrating clear learning pathways, career development visibility, and recognition of prior learning — create value that extends beyond course delivery. Positioning training programmes as part of a broader employer value proposition (EVP) strategy, and building employer partnership models where banks co-fund and co-market programmes, aligns with the approach already taken by 80% of South Africa's leading employers.[^7]

***

## Conclusion

South African banking faces a skills emergency that conventional education pipelines cannot resolve within the three-year horizon that matters most to the sector. The convergence of AI deployment at scale, an intensifying regulatory environment, a cybersecurity crisis, and persistent brain drain has created structural shortfalls across at least six distinct skills clusters — and the pace of change is accelerating rather than slowing. Banks spent R5.648 billion on skills development in 2023, and the SARB increased its own training spend to R159.7 million — 7% of total payroll — in 2024/25, clear signals that the sector is willing to invest in closing these gaps.[^29][^26]

For a training provider with deep banking-sector relationships and the capability to design contextualised, regulatory-aligned programmes, the strategic opportunity is significant. Prioritising applied AI and data analytics, cybersecurity awareness, regulatory compliance (particularly AML/CFT and Basel IV), AI governance, and transformation leadership — in formats that are modular, CPD-accredited, and BANKSETA-aligned — will best position the organisation to address the most urgent and well-funded needs in the sector over the next three years.
