Executive Summary

Since January 2024, South African banking has entered a period of unprecedented regulatory intensity around digital capability. Three regulatory changes stand out for their combined breadth, binding force, and skills implications: the Joint Standard on Cybersecurity and Cyber Resilience (JS2 of 2024), the Joint Standard on IT Governance and Risk Management (JS1 of 2023), and the Basel III Post-Crisis Reforms (effective July 2025).

Together, these instruments require banks to build layered digital competencies — from board-level technology literacy to specialist quantitative modelling — at a time when South Africa faces acute shortages in cybersecurity professionals, IT governance practitioners, and data scientists. The gap between regulatory expectation and workforce reality is widening.

Methodology

This report synthesises publicly available regulatory texts, supervisory communications, sector skills plans, industry surveys, and consulting firm analyses published between January 2024 and April 2026.

The three regulatory changes were selected and ranked using a scoring framework based on three criteria, each scored on a five-point scale (maximum composite: 15):

What It Requires

Issued jointly by the Prudential Authority and FSCA, this Standard applies to all financial institutions — banks, insurers, retirement funds, fund administrators, and collective investment scheme managers. It establishes minimum requirements across the full cybersecurity lifecycle:

  • A cybersecurity strategy and framework
  • Identification and classification of business processes and information assets
  • Annual cybersecurity awareness training for governing bodies and all users
  • Data loss prevention policies
  • Tested, auditable incident response plans with executive involvement
  • A dedicated cybersecurity officer (recommended, distinct from IT manager)

What It Requires

Already in force, JS1 requires governing bodies to approve the institution's IT strategy and mandates comprehensive oversight of IT internal controls and risk management. The IT function must be headed by a qualified individual who is part of senior management.

A comprehensive IT governance and risk management training programme is mandated for all levels, reviewed annually.

The Skills Gap

The challenge here is diffusion, not novelty. IT governance is an established discipline, but extending these competencies to boards of smaller institutions, and exercising the judgement required by the Standard's principles-based approach, is where the gap lies.

Key Relationship
JS1 is foundational to JS2. The IT governance architecture provides the structural framework within which cybersecurity capabilities must operate. Failure to comply with JS1 cascades into non-compliance with JS2 and emerging cloud and AI standards.

What It Requires

The final tranche of internationally agreed prudential standards: revised credit risk approaches (standardised and IRB), a new operational risk framework, an updated leverage ratio, and the output floor (phasing in at 60%). Compliance requires significant data infrastructure and risk modelling systems upgrades.

Skills Demanded

Systems integration

The Pipeline Problem

The skills gap here is structural and worsening. Declining matric pass rates in mathematics and physical sciences are shrinking the pipeline of students who can enter quantitative university programmes.

Structural Constraint
A student who does not pass matric mathematics at the required level cannot enter the university programmes that produce risk modellers and data scientists. This is not a gap that corporate training can close — it is a pipeline problem rooted in the education system. McKinsey projects a persistent talent shortage through at least 2030.

The Cumulative Effect

Layered Demands, Compounding Gaps

These regulatory changes impose layered and interdependent demands on the same institutions, often on the same teams, within overlapping timeframes. A mid-tier bank in mid-2025 must simultaneously:

  • Maintain IT governance architecture under JS1 (in force since November 2024)
  • Achieve cybersecurity compliance under JS2 (June 2025)
  • Complete Basel III systems upgrades (July 2025)
  • Continue meeting POPIA, cloud, and conduct obligations

The IT governance professional, the cybersecurity specialist, and the quantitative risk modeller are different people with different training — but they must work within integrated frameworks. Scarcity in any one domain creates bottlenecks that affect all the others.

King V Amplification
The King V Code (effective January 2026) establishes data, technology, and AI as a standalone governance pillar: boards must be "technologically literate, not just financially literate." Directors can no longer delegate technology oversight entirely to management.

The Pipeline Problem

The BankSETA Sector Skills Plans identify cybersecurity, data analytics, and digital platform management as the most critical gaps. The Institute of Bankers in South Africa has identified five key future banker skills:

Cybersecurity & Data Protection
Data Analytics & Interpretation
Digital Platform Management
Customer Experience Design
Regulatory Technology (RegTech)

PwC, EY, KPMG, Deloitte, Accenture, and McKinsey all flag the convergence of regulatory technology demands and workforce skills gaps as a defining challenge for South African banking. IT spending is tracking above inflation, but banks struggle to attract and retain qualified talent in a globally competitive market.

The root of the problem extends beyond banking. Declining secondary-school pass rates in mathematics and physical sciences constrain the pipeline of students entering quantitative and technical university programmes. This structural limitation implies the skills gap will persist — and potentially widen — through the end of the decade.

Close Calls

Two regulatory developments came close to inclusion but fell short:

POPIA Amendments (scored 11/15) — High urgency (immediately effective, no transition period) but incremental skills demands on institutions that have already invested in POPIA compliance since 2021.

AI in Financial Sector Report (scored 8/15) — Highest future potential. Found that 53% of financial sector staff lack AI training and governance frameworks are immature. However, it carries zero binding regulatory force today. When binding AI standards emerge, this area will almost certainly enter the top tier.

Limitations

  • Data availability — relies on publicly available sources as of April 2026; internal compliance data and supervisory assessments are not reflected
  • Methodological variation — skills gap statistics from different sources use different methodologies, sample frames, and timeframes
  • Ranking sensitivity — different criteria weighting could yield different rankings; weighting future impact more heavily would elevate AI governance
  • Regulatory pipeline — binding AI governance standards, a cloud computing standard, and the COFI Bill could substantially alter the landscape
  • Sector focus — analysis focuses on banking specifically; dynamics in insurance, retirement funds, and other sub-sectors may differ
