Understand and Explore
Getting set up

Claude lives in the cloud. Your conversations, projects, and settings are all stored on Anthropic’s servers, not on your machine — so whichever way you access Claude, you’re working in the same place, with one account. That means you can start on the web today, switch to the desktop app next week, and everything is exactly where you left it.

The default: start with the web. Go to claude.ai and create an account. Clean, safe, nothing on your local machine. This is where you’ll spend the next week.

When you’re ready: the desktop app. Download it from claude.ai. It’s the same Claude, a bit more convenient — sits in your taskbar, easier to make a daily habit. In chat mode it behaves identically to the web. You’ll notice Code and Cowork in the sidebar — leave those for now, we’ll get to them later in the course.

One practical note: if your organisation doesn’t yet have an enterprise AI agreement, use a personal account for now. We’ll cover the security and policy considerations shortly.

Subscription

Free is fine for trying Claude out. But if you’ve decided you want to build a real working relationship with it, get Pro. It’s $20 a month and it’s probably the highest-return $20 you’ll spend on your professional development this year.

Here’s what Pro gets you:

The habit argument: usage limits on Free don’t just inconvenience you, they break the habit loop at exactly the moment you’re trying to form it. Pro removes that friction entirely.

QS2: Set up your Projects

QuickStart
  1. Click on Projects in the left sidebar, then “New project”.
  2. Give it a name and a description (“what are you trying to achieve”). This description is for you and coworkers; it is not referenced by Claude.
  3. Populate Instructions. This is perhaps the key piece of information. This will guide Claude in any of the Chats created under this Project. It can be very detailed, if appropriate.
  4. Populate Files, by drag and drop or upload. These files will be referenced by every chat in the project.
Understand and Explore
Projects

By default every conversation in Claude is isolated — it doesn’t know what you discussed yesterday or last week. Projects fix that.

A Project is a persistent workspace with four elements:

  • A name and purpose — what the project is for
  • Instructions — how you want Claude to behave within this project: your role, your preferences, your rules
  • A knowledge base — files, documents, and reference material you upload once and Claude draws on whenever you need it. You can also connect GitHub repositories and Google Drive directly
  • Chats — all the conversations you have within the project, kept together in one place

What this means in practice: instead of starting every conversation from scratch, you build a Project for each significant area of your work — a client, a research topic, a recurring task. Over time the Project accumulates context and becomes genuinely useful as a working environment rather than just a chat interface.

Organising your chat history matters. A Project is only as useful as the discipline you bring to it. Name your files descriptively, keep your knowledge base current, and think of the Project as a living workspace rather than a filing cabinet. Unstructured chat history quickly becomes noise.

A note on collaboration: sharing Projects with colleagues requires a Team or Enterprise subscription. On a personal Pro account your Projects are private.

Tips
  • Instructions and files that are only relevant to a particular chat can be inserted directly into those Chats.
  • A Project also has a memory, populated by Claude as the Project evolves. But you can edit the memory yourself by adding stuff or removing stuff. While “Instructions” and “Files” are the most obvious resources available to Claude in a Project, “Memory” is a powerful tool, especially in longer projects. If you are planning to build a Project that you use repeatedly over time, spend time finding out exactly how “Project memory” works (ask Claude!).
  • You need to think carefully about exactly how a Project is going to be used. You may want to start small and let it grow. Don’t be afraid to start a new Project if you have misconceived the first iteration.
  • You can manage your Project space as it grows, by favouriting, deleting and archiving.

QS3: Get acquainted with Claude’s core functionality

Understand and Explore
Artifacts

Sometimes Claude’s response isn’t just text in the conversation — it produces a self-contained piece of work: a document, a table, a piece of code, a formatted report, an interactive page. These are called Artifacts and they appear in a separate panel alongside the conversation.

Why it matters: an Artifact isn’t just a response, it’s a working document. You can ask Claude to revise it, extend it, reformat it — all while keeping the conversation going. The Artifact updates in place rather than producing a new block of text each time. You can also switch between the finished output and the underlying code if you want to see how it was built.

What triggers an Artifact: Claude decides based on the nature of the output. Longer structured documents, code, and formatted content typically appear as Artifacts automatically.

Artifacts can be shared or published directly from Claude.

Adding stuff

One of Claude’s most underused capabilities: you don’t have to explain everything in words. You can bring material directly into the conversation.

  • Typed context — paste in a document, a data extract, an email thread, a set of notes. Claude reads it and works with it. This is often faster and more accurate than trying to describe something in your own words.
  • Uploaded files — attach files directly to a conversation. Supported formats include PDF, DOCX, CSV, TXT, and common image formats like PNG and JPEG.
  • Screenshots — you can paste or upload a screenshot directly into the conversation. Useful for sharing something visual without having to describe it — a chart, a UI, a document you can’t easily export.

The practical habit: before you ask Claude to help with something, ask yourself whether there’s a document, file, or screenshot that would give it better raw material than your description alone. The easiest and most intuitive methods are simply to copy and paste from your clipboard, or drag and drop a file, directly into the conversation.

Tips
  • When Claude produces an Artifact, don’t just copy and paste it and move on. Stay in the conversation and refine it until it’s actually what you need.
  • Even if Claude has decided not to show something as an Artifact, you can ask it to.
  • Artifacts are “updated in place” — so there is only one version on display. But there is an updated version number and you can go back to earlier versions.
  • Artifacts can also be interactive. For this and other reasons, they can be used as mini-demos, in place, without having to create a demo page.
  • As the course progresses, you’ll pick up more powerful ways to bring material into Claude: Connectors give Claude persistent access to cloud services; Cowork and Code give Claude direct access to approved local directories. These become important when you’re working with large datasets or information that changes frequently. For now, copy/paste and drag/drop will take you a long way.
  • Note that as you start to use Projects, persistent memory, and Skills, Claude will often know a lot of your context before you even start a new chat.

Connectors

So far everything you’ve brought into a conversation you’ve had to actively add — typed context, uploaded files. Connectors are different: they give Claude persistent access to external services so the information is already there when you need it.

Connectors don’t just let Claude read from external services — they allow Claude to act on your behalf too. That’s the shift from assistant to informed collaborator.

  • Web connectors — connect cloud services like Google Drive, Gmail, and Google Calendar. Set up once in Settings, available across all your chats and Projects
  • Desktop extensions — connect Claude to things on your local machine: your file system, local applications, and tools like the Chrome extension that lets Claude see your open tabs. These are available through the desktop app

Understand and Explore
Basic chat technique

The single biggest mistake new users make: type a question, read the answer, close the chat. That’s using Claude as a search engine. It’s not what it’s for.

Three things to give Claude before every task:

  • Context — who you are, what you’re working on, why it matters
  • Task — what you actually want Claude to do
  • Guidelines — tone, format, length, constraints

You don’t need all three every time, but the habit of thinking in these terms immediately improves your results.

Stay in the conversation. Claude’s first response is a starting point, not a finished product. Push back, redirect, ask for alternatives. Sometimes, start from scratch in a new chat. The value compounds as the conversation develops.

Fluency is not accuracy. Claude writes clearly and confidently even when it’s wrong. A polished answer is not a correct answer. Verify anything that matters.

One last thing

Open Claude before you open anything else in the morning.

Not because you’ll always have something specific to do with it. But because the habit of reaching for it first — before email, before your task list, before your browser — is what turns an occasional tool into a working partner.

The people who get the most out of Claude aren’t the ones who use it for the biggest tasks. They’re the ones who use it constantly, for small things and large, until the instinct to reach for it becomes automatic.

AI Security Essentials

A take-home guide for course participants

Using AI tools like Claude as a daily driver is low-risk for most people — but a few simple habits make it genuinely safe. This guide separates what actually matters from what you can stop worrying about.

1. External Threats — Lower Risk Than You Think

When you use a web-based AI tool like Claude.ai, you are trusting a well-resourced technology company to handle security on your behalf. Counterintuitively, this is safer than managing software locally — you are not responsible for keeping anything patched, configured, or updated.

Your conversation data is transmitted over an encrypted connection (TLS), so interception in transit is not a realistic risk. Anthropic’s servers are not a likely target for someone trying to steal your specific data.

The real external risks are account-level, not platform-level:

  • Phishing — fake login pages designed to steal your credentials
  • Password reuse — if one account is breached, others become vulnerable
  • Device compromise — malware on your machine captures data before it reaches any server

Action: Two things to do now

  • Install Bitwarden (free, open source). One strong master password protects all your accounts, and you stop reusing passwords across AI tools and other services.
  • Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) on your Claude.ai account. This prevents account takeover even if your password is stolen.

2. Confidential Data — This Is Where Judgement Matters

When you paste text into an AI chat window, you are sending it to a third-party server. For most personal use this is fine. For work data, it requires thought.

Ask yourself one question before pasting anything:

“Would I be comfortable if my employer saw exactly what I just pasted into this chat?”

If the answer is no — or even maybe — use made-up data instead. You can learn everything you need to learn with synthetic examples. Real work data adds risk and is rarely necessary.

Things to be aware of:

  • Many employers now have AI use policies. Check yours. Breaching it could have professional consequences regardless of any actual data leak.
  • Regulated industries (healthcare, legal, finance, government) have specific data handling obligations that may apply even to informal use.
  • By default, AI providers may use conversations to improve their models. You can opt out of this in Claude.ai settings — worth doing as a matter of routine.
  • If your organisation needs to use AI with sensitive data legitimately, enterprise tiers (such as Claude for Work) have different contractual data protections.

3. A Built-In Safety Prompt

Claude.ai lets you set a permanent system prompt — background instructions that apply to every conversation. As part of your setup, we will add an instruction that prompts Claude to flag inputs that look like real personal or work data (lists of names with numbers, email addresses, client records, etc.) and ask you whether you want to continue.

This is not a technical guardrail — it will not catch everything. But it creates a useful pause at exactly the moment you might otherwise act without thinking. Think of it as a seatbelt reminder chime, not a seatbelt.

Your Security Checklist

  • Install Bitwarden and create a strong master password
  • Enable 2FA on Claude.ai (and any other AI accounts you create)
  • Check the training data opt-out setting in Claude.ai preferences
  • Find out if your employer has an AI use policy
  • Default to synthetic or anonymised data when practising — not real work data
  • Apply the one-question test before pasting anything sensitive

1. The Skilled User Frame

Session 3: Deep Research in the Wild

As AI tools become more capable, the gap between casual users and skilled users widens. Skilled users are not necessarily more technical — they are more deliberate. They think clearly about three things:

Input — the material you bring to the conversation: documents, data, files, screenshots. The raw substance the model works with.

Direction — everything you say to the model: the question, the context, the instructions, the constraints, the format, the iteration. Direction is how you guide the model toward useful work.

Evaluation — how you assess what comes back. Not “does this look good?” but structured, criteria-driven judgment applied consistently.

This session focuses on Evaluation. Direction becomes the focus of Session 4. Input becomes the focus of Session 5.

A note on why evaluation matters: AI outputs are increasingly polished and confident in tone. The easier they are to read, the harder it is to spot what is missing, overstated, or wrong. Evaluation is the skill that keeps you in control of the work — and it transfers across every tool you will ever use.

2. Key Concepts

Session 3: Deep Research in the Wild

Deep Research

Deep Research is a specific product feature — available in Claude, ChatGPT, and Perplexity — not simply a description of researching with AI. It was among the first applications outside of coding to combine three capabilities that had previously operated separately:

  • Extended thinking
  • Agentic behaviour
  • Tool use

When you trigger Deep Research, you are not sending a prompt to a language model and waiting for a response. You are initiating an autonomous research process that can run for several minutes, visit dozens of sources, and make hundreds of decisions before producing its output.

Extended Thinking / Chain-of-Thought Reasoning

Standard AI responses are generated fluidly, with no visible reasoning step. Extended thinking changes this: the model is given time and compute to plan its approach, break the problem down, and reason through it before producing output. Where visible — as in Claude’s thinking indicator or ChatGPT’s activity log — you are watching the model work, not just watching it write.

This is inference-time computation: the model doing more work per question rather than simply being a larger model.

Agentic Behaviour

An agent is a system that pursues a goal by taking a sequence of actions, using tools, and making decisions along the way. Deep Research tools behave agentically: they decide what to search for, choose which sources to read, extract what seems relevant, identify gaps, and search again. They are not following a fixed script — they are planning and replanning in response to what they find.

Tool Use

Agentic behaviour requires tools. In Deep Research, the primary tool is web search: the model calls the search tool, receives results, decides what to do with them, and calls it again as needed. The search–analyse–replan cycle — visible in ChatGPT’s activity log — can repeat dozens of times in a single research run. This is the same architecture that underlies the more powerful agentic systems we will see in Session 6.

3. The Evaluation Rubric

Session 3: Deep Research in the Wild

Evaluation is not a general impression formed after reading. It is a structured assessment applied consistently against explicit criteria. The rubric below organises those criteria into three levels.

You will use this rubric for your homework after this session. You will use it again in Sessions 4 and 5 to evaluate work you have produced yourself.

Process Quality — how did it research?

  • Breadth of sources — did it draw on a wide range of relevant sources, or rely heavily on a narrow base?
  • Authority of sources — are the sources credible, current, and appropriate for the question?
  • Geographic and sectoral specificity — did it find sources directly relevant to the context, or did it generalise from elsewhere?
  • Transparency of reasoning — can you see what it did, what it chose, and why?

Content Quality — what did it find?

  • Quality of insight extraction — did it draw meaningful conclusions from its sources, or summarise superficially?
  • Domain coverage — did it address the full scope of the question, or leave significant areas thin or absent?
  • Contextual accuracy — did it handle the specific regulatory, institutional, and market context correctly?
  • Logical coherence — do the findings hold together as an argument, or sit as disconnected observations?

Output Quality — what did it produce?

  • Structure and navigability — is the report organised clearly and easy to use?
  • Specificity of recommendations — are recommendations actionable, or generic enough to apply to any situation?
  • Caveats and limitations — does the report acknowledge what it does not know, where data is incomplete, or where claims are uncertain?
  • Fitness for purpose — could you hand this report to a senior client and defend every claim in it?

That last question is the one that matters most for the kind of work this course is built around. A report that looks authoritative but cannot survive scrutiny is not a research output — it is a liability.

1. Where We Are

In Session 3 you watched three canned deep research tools tackle the same question and saw that their outputs -- however polished -- contained hidden weaknesses that only systematic evaluation exposed. The homework question was: what would you need to change, add, or verify before you could stand behind one of those reports?

This session answers that question. It walks through a complete directed research process -- from source discovery to a finished report -- designed so that every step is transparent, every source is chosen deliberately, and the final output is defensible under scrutiny.

In IDE terms, Session 3 focused on Evaluation. This session focuses on Direction. You will see that Direction is not a single skill but a repertoire -- different stages of a research process need different kinds of instruction, and understanding why each instruction is shaped the way it is matters more than memorising templates.

2. The Process Architecture

The research process demonstrated in this session has eight steps, organised as a funnel: wide at the top (casting a broad net for sources), narrowing through quality checks and human curation, synthesised into themes, and shaped into a defensible report.

  • DR sweep -- Run Deep Research on the question. Harvest the source list. Discard the report.
  • Training knowledge sweep -- Ask Claude to identify authoritative sources missing from the DR list, drawing on its training knowledge.
  • Human knowledge sweep -- The researcher adds sources from their own domain expertise.
  • Source quality check -- Claude checks each source for accessibility, recency, credibility, and relevance. Output: an annotated audit.
  • Human curation -- The researcher reviews the audit, ranks sources, and produces a clean curated list.
  • Literature review -- Claude reads and summarises each curated source individually, focused on the research question. No synthesis yet.
  • Analytical synthesis -- Claude organises the evidence by theme, rates evidence strength, surfaces tensions and gaps. No recommendations yet.
  • Report -- Claude produces a structured, evidence-graded report shaped by a detailed instruction specifying audience, structure, and evidence-handling rules.

Each step produces a distinct product that feeds the next. Each requires a different kind of instruction. The process can be run end to end in a Claude Project, with all intermediate products preserved as a transparent audit trail.

3. Source Discovery and Curation

Canned deep research tools select their own sources autonomously. You have no control over what they find, what they miss, or what they prioritise. The source discovery pipeline gives you that control.

3.1 -- Deep Research sweep

Run Deep Research on the research question with minimal direction. The purpose is to use DR as a search engine with judgment -- it will visit dozens or hundreds of pages and identify the ones it considers most relevant. When it finishes, extract the list of sources it cited. The report itself is not the product of this step; the source list is.

In the session demo, DR cited 73 sources (while visiting over 500 pages during its search). The distinction between pages visited and sources cited is worth noting -- the large number displayed during the run does not mean all of those pages ended up in the report.

3.2 -- Training knowledge sweep

In a standard Claude chat (not DR), provide the research question and the DR source list. Ask Claude to identify authoritative sources that are relevant but missing from the list. This taps a different knowledge base: the model's awareness of institutions, recurring reports, key authors, and regulatory bodies from its training data, which may not have appeared in DR's real-time web search.

In the session demo, this sweep produced approximately 40 additional sources and identified three significant gaps in the DR list: the Big 5 banks' own annual integrated reports were entirely absent, some sources cited in the DR report's body text did not appear in its own source list, and an entire category of practical data (recruitment firm salary guides) was missing.

3.3 -- Human knowledge sweep

The researcher adds sources from their own domain knowledge: reports they have seen cited, institutions they know are active in the space, niche publications, known experts. This is where the researcher's professional expertise enters the process. No AI can substitute for deep domain familiarity.

3.4 -- Source quality check

Give Claude the combined source list (from all three sweeps) and ask it to check each source for: URL accessibility, publication date, whether it is a primary or secondary source, institutional credibility of the publisher, and direct relevance to the research question. Ask for the output as a structured table (CSV or spreadsheet) with a recommendation column: Keep, Query, or Remove.

In the session demo, 115 sources were checked. Key findings: 61 recommended to Keep, 40 to Query, 14 to Remove. Only 62 URLs were confirmed live. Of the 42 training knowledge sources, only 18 had confirmed live URLs -- confirming that training knowledge produces good institutional names but unreliable URLs.

A practical note: checking 115 URLs one by one hits Claude's tool call limits. The workaround is clicking "continue" when prompted. For production use, a script that handles mechanical URL checks and reserves AI for judgment tasks (credibility, relevance) would be more efficient.

3.5 -- Human curation

The researcher reviews the annotated source audit and makes the final decisions: which sources to keep, which to discard, and how to rank them. A simple ranking system works well: A (essential -- must be covered in depth), B (important -- should be covered), C (useful -- reference if relevant), D (ignore).

In the session demo, a tight filter was applied: keep only sources with Status = Live and Recommendation = Keep. This produced 40 sources with an even split of 20 primary and 20 secondary sources -- a clean, defensible list.

This is the step where human judgment is irreplaceable. The AI can check URLs and assess credibility, but deciding which sources matter most for this specific question and this specific audience is a human call.

The product: A transparent, quality-checked, human-curated source list. Every source is there for a reason. Every source has been verified. If a client asks "why did you use this source?" or "why didn't you use that one?", you can answer.

A note on recurring research: The source list becomes a maintained asset. The expensive multi-sweep discovery runs once. On subsequent runs -- monthly, quarterly -- the task is simpler: check the canonical list for health and search for new publications from known institutions. The process gets faster and the list gets better over time.

4. Literature Review

With the curated source list in hand, the next step is to find out what the sources actually say. The instruction asks Claude to read each source and produce a focused summary, concentrating on content relevant to the research question. Crucially, it asks for individual summaries -- no integration, no synthesis, no attempt to answer the research question. The product is a structured digest: what each source says, on its own terms.

This step requires web access (Claude needs to visit the URLs and read the content). In the session demo, DR mode was used for this purpose. The instruction explicitly told DR not to search for additional sources beyond the curated list. DR acknowledged the constraint but visited over 400 pages during the run -- it did not fully respect the process constraint, although the final output reported on only the 40 specified sources.

The lesson: DR may not respect process constraints even when it respects output constraints. For production work where strict source control matters, standard chat with web search may give more reliable control.

5. Analytical Synthesis

The synthesis is the analytical backbone of the process. It takes the 40 individual source summaries and reorganises them by theme, answering four questions:

  • What are the major themes that emerge across the sources? Which sources support each theme, and how strongly?
  • Where do sources present conflicting data, different figures, or divergent assessments of the same issue?
  • What significant gaps remain in the evidence base -- areas the research question requires but the sources do not adequately address?
  • Where is the evidence strong (multiple authoritative primary sources converging) versus where does it rest on a single source or on secondary reporting of the same underlying data?

The instruction for the synthesis explicitly states: do not make recommendations. Organise and assess. This separation matters. If you go straight from source summaries to a report, the AI will do the synthesis implicitly and you lose visibility into the analytical layer. You cannot see whether a recommendation is well-supported or built on a single data point.

Key findings from the session demo synthesis:

  • 11 themes identified, each with an explicit evidence-strength rating
  • Cybersecurity was the best-evidenced shortage, supported by multiple independent primary sources. But the specific figure of a 30,000-person deficit came from a single paywalled commercial report whose methodology could not be verified.
  • AI/ML demand had strong directional consensus from bank spending data and hiring patterns, but no source provided a quantified estimate of the gap
  • Two articles that appeared to be independent sources were authored by the same organisation (Fortinet) citing the same underlying data -- what looked like corroboration was actually a single evidence stream
  • IT spending figures for one bank appeared at four different values across sources, due to different reporting boundaries (with/without staff costs, group vs. SA operations) -- flagged as a tension, not silently resolved
  • Nine significant evidence gaps were identified, including the absence of the Big 5 banks' own annual reports, salary benchmarking data, and education pipeline data

The synthesis distinguishes between what the evidence supports, what it suggests but cannot confirm, and what it does not address at all. That distinction is what makes the final report defensible.

6. The Report

The report instruction is the most detailed and opinionated in the process. It specifies:

  • Audience and purpose -- who will read this and what it is for. In the demo: a senior leadership team at a Big 5 bank, with the report demonstrating analytical capability.
  • Explicit structure -- the sections the report must contain, each with a clear function. In the demo: executive summary, skills landscape, regulatory drivers, supply-side problem, bank responses, evidence gaps, and a prioritised training framework.
  • Evidence-handling rules -- where evidence is strong, say so. Where it rests on a single source, say that too. Do not present weak evidence with the same confidence as strong evidence. Where the synthesis identified tensions, acknowledge them rather than resolving them silently.
  • The no-filler rule -- no generic paragraphs about the importance of digital transformation. Every paragraph must contain a specific claim, a specific piece of evidence, or a specific judgment.

The report produced in the session demo ran to approximately 11 pages. It ranked five skills shortage areas by evidence strength and strategic urgency. It presented a regulatory drivers table covering six simultaneous regulatory changes with timelines and evidence ratings. It acknowledged what the analysis could not tell us. And it closed with a substantive argument about the kind of training investment the evidence supports.

The critical comparison with Session 3: The canned reports from Session 3 are slicker and read more fluently. But this report knows what it does not know. It distinguishes strong evidence from weak. It surfaces contradictions rather than hiding them. It has a transparent, auditable source base. It is defensible under scrutiny -- and the canned reports are not.

That standard -- defensible under scrutiny -- is the core argument of this session. Everything in the process contributes to it: the multi-sweep source discovery catches what any single tool misses, the quality audit removes unreliable sources before they can contaminate the analysis, the human curation applies domain judgment, the separated synthesis makes the evidence structure visible, and the evidence-graded report inherits all of that transparency.

7. The Direction Repertoire

One of the key lessons of this session is that Direction is not a single skill. Different stages of a research process require fundamentally different kinds of instruction. The process demonstrated today used six distinct types:

  • Minimal direction (DR sweep) -- deliberately under-specified, to see what the tool finds on its own without constraining the search
  • Knowledge elicitation (training knowledge sweep) -- asking Claude to critique a list against its own training, drawing out knowledge that would not surface through web search
  • Structured audit (source quality check) -- explicit column definitions, a specified output format, and actionable recommendations for each item
  • Constraint instruction (literature review) -- telling Claude what not to do: do not add sources, do not synthesise, do not answer the question
  • Analytical instruction (synthesis) -- organise and assess the evidence, surface tensions and gaps, but do not draw conclusions or make recommendations
  • Opinionated instruction (report) -- a detailed specification of audience, structure, tone, evidence-handling rules, and quality standards

Each instruction is shaped by what that step needs to produce. A minimal instruction would be wrong for the report. An opinionated instruction would constrain the DR sweep prematurely. Understanding why each instruction is shaped the way it is -- not just what it says -- is the skill that transfers across every research process you will design.

S5: Deep Research 3: Offline Data

A Practitioner’s Guide to LLMs

What They Are and How to Enhance Them

Opening

To use any tool well, you need an accurate mental model of what it is. This is especially true for LLMs, because the two most common mental models — “it’s just autocomplete” and “it’s a superintelligence” — both lead to predictable mistakes. We’re going to build a better one.

The structure is simple: first, what kind of intelligence is an LLM? Then, how do we enhance it?

A. What Kind of Intelligence Is an LLM?

A1. It’s a neural network

A neural network is a system of interconnected computational units, loosely inspired by neurons in the brain. Early AI systems — chess engines, expert systems — were rule-based: a developer wrote explicit rules, and the machine followed them. Neural networks work differently: instead of following rules, they learn from examples. Through a process called gradient descent, the system repeatedly adjusts its internal connections, taking small steps in the direction of better performance — like finding the lowest point in a hilly landscape by always stepping downhill — until it gets reliably better at the task.

This is deep learning — neural networks with many layers, where each layer learns to recognise increasingly abstract patterns. Early layers might detect edges in an image; deeper layers recognise faces. The key insight that unlocked practical deep learning was not new mathematics — it was scale. When you combine this architecture with enough data and enough computing power, something remarkable happens: capability emerges that nobody explicitly programmed.

A2. It’s a language model

The second thing to understand is what this particular neural network was trained to do — and what that training produced.

The story begins with machine translation. In the 2010s, neural networks trained on parallel texts in different languages began outperforming rule-based translation systems. Researchers noticed something interesting: to translate well, the model had to develop rich internal representations of meaning, not just word-for-word mappings. Language was turning out to be a surprisingly powerful training signal.

The decisive architectural breakthrough came in 2017. A paper called Attention Is All You Need introduced the attention mechanism and the Transformer architecture — a design that lets the model consider every word in a passage in relation to every other word simultaneously, rather than processing language sequentially. Because attention allows for parallel processing in a way that pre-Transformer models did not, this made it possible to train on vastly larger datasets in parallel. You could go very big, very fast.

What followed was a decision to train these networks on an enormous fraction of the written internet — books, articles, code, conversation. The result is a model that has learned the statistical distribution of human language: what kinds of text follow from what other kinds of text, across essentially the full breadth of recorded human knowledge.

This is the key conceptual move: an LLM is not a database. It doesn’t store facts and retrieve them. It has compressed the patterns of an enormous corpus into a set of internal weights, and when you prompt it, it generates a response by sampling from those learned patterns — predicting what a good continuation would look like.

This is genuinely powerful. Predicting what a good answer looks like, across the full range of human knowledge, requires rich internal representations of that knowledge. But it is also the source of the model’s most important failure mode: hallucination. The model isn’t lying when it produces a plausible-sounding but false citation. It has no mechanism to distinguish “I know this” from “this fits the pattern of something I’d know.” It generates fluently in both cases. Confidence is not a signal of accuracy. This is not a bug to be patched — it is a property of the architecture. The right response is not to distrust the tool, but to understand when verification matters.

Bridge

So: a neural network, trained on human language, that models statistical distributions rather than storing facts. This base intelligence has characteristic strengths — fluency, breadth, pattern recognition across domains, tireless availability — and characteristic weaknesses — hallucination, no persistent memory, no reliable self-knowledge about the limits of its knowledge.

Enhancement is the natural response to that profile. And the idea of enhancing a base intelligence is not new. We have always built on top of the base intelligence we were given — through language, writing, mathematics, computation, and now AI. Each layer extended what came before. The same logic applies here.

A brief aside — and then back to work

We should note something that is easy to miss in the rush to use these tools: we now live alongside a genuinely new kind of intelligence. Not a smarter human. Not a dumber human. Something built on entirely different foundations — designed rather than evolved, trained on human artefacts rather than shaped by survival pressures, with a completely different profile of strengths and weaknesses.

We have, until very recently, shared the world only with other evolved intelligences — humans and animals, all shaped by the same basic pressures. Artificial intelligences are categorically different. They are behaviourally familiar, because they were trained on everything we wrote, but structurally alien.

The most useful approach is not to rank them against human intelligence, but to understand them on their own terms. What is this thing actually good at? What does it genuinely struggle with? That’s the question that makes you a better user — and it’s the question the rest of this session is designed to answer.

B. Enhancing the Intelligence

B1. Model enhancements — what the builders do

The base model — trained on the internet, predicting next tokens — is powerful but raw. It will complete any text in whatever direction the patterns suggest, including harmful, false, or simply unhelpful directions. Several layers of enhancement are applied before you interact with it.

Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) and related fine-tuning techniques shape the model’s behaviour: making it helpful, honest, and safe. Human raters evaluate outputs; the model learns to produce outputs that score well. This is why Claude behaves like an assistant rather than a raw text predictor.

Explicit reasoning — sometimes called chain-of-thought — is the discovery that models perform dramatically better on complex problems when they are prompted, or trained, to think step by step before answering. Rather than jumping to a completion, the model works through intermediate steps. This is the basis of the “extended thinking” modes you may have encountered.

Inference-time scaling is the most recent development: rather than just training bigger models, you run more computation at the moment of answering — letting the model explore multiple reasoning paths, check its own work, or run longer internal deliberation. This has driven significant capability jumps in the last year.

Most recently, agentic capacity itself is becoming a builder-level enhancement — shipped as part of the foundation model rather than constructed by users. Claude Code, which autonomously plans and executes complex coding tasks, and Cowork, which automates file and task management for non-developers, are current examples. The boundary between “what the model can do” and “what you have to build” is shifting rapidly — a point we will return to.

B2. User techniques — what you can do

Context is the most important and most underused lever. The model has no persistent memory — everything it knows about your situation is what you put in front of it in the current conversation. Rich context produces dramatically better output. Most new users underinvest here.

Prompt engineering is the practice of structuring your inputs to guide the model’s thinking and outputs. It ranges from simple (be specific, give examples, specify the format you want) to sophisticated (role assignment, chain-of-thought prompting, system prompts).

Tools and skills extend the model beyond its training data. A model with access to web search, code execution, or file systems can act in the world, not just generate text.

Agents are systems where the model plans and executes multi-step tasks autonomously — breaking a complex goal into steps, using tools along the way, and iterating until the job is done. This is where the technology is moving fastest, with agentic capabilities increasingly being built into the underlying models and agentic applications such as Claude Code.

Coda: Why Understanding Matters More Than Technique

There is a pattern worth naming before we finish.

Since ChatGPT went public in late 2022, the capability of foundation models has risen steeply and continuously. Things that required significant custom engineering 18 months ago are now handled natively — by the model itself, or by tools like Claude Code, or by agents that no longer need elaborate custom infrastructure to orchestrate. People and organisations that invested heavily in building technical workarounds for the limitations of 2023-era models have repeatedly found that investment overtaken by model improvements. The specific workaround becomes unnecessary. The Python pipeline someone spent months building gets replaced by a conversation.

This creates a real risk for anyone trying to develop AI capability: following the hype into deep investment in today’s limitations, only to find the model outgrows them in six months.

The antidote is literacy. Understanding what the model actually is — how it learns, what it’s good at, where it fails and why — compounds over time. It makes you a better user of today’s models and a faster adapter to tomorrow’s. Specific techniques become obsolete. The understanding that lets you evaluate and adopt new techniques does not.

That is what this course is designed to build. Not a toolkit for 2026. A foundation that gets more valuable as the models get more capable.

What followed was a decision to train these networks on an enormous fraction of the written internet -- books, articles, code, conversation. The result is a model that has learned the statistical distribution of human language: what kinds of text follow from what other kinds of text, across essentially the full breadth of recorded human knowledge.

This is the key conceptual move: an LLM is not a database. It doesn't store facts and retrieve them. It has compressed the patterns of an enormous corpus into a set of internal weights, and when you prompt it, it generates a response by sampling from those learned patterns -- predicting what a good continuation would look like.

This is genuinely powerful. Predicting what a good answer looks like, across the full range of human knowledge, requires rich internal representations of that knowledge. But it is also the source of the model's most important failure mode: hallucination. The model isn't lying when it produces a plausible-sounding but false citation. It has no mechanism to distinguish "I know this" from "this fits the pattern of something I'd know." It generates fluently in both cases. Confidence is not a signal of accuracy. This is not a bug to be patched -- it is a property of the architecture. The right response is not to distrust the tool, but to understand when verification matters.

Bridge

So: a neural network, trained on human language, that models statistical distributions rather than storing facts. This base intelligence has characteristic strengths -- fluency, breadth, pattern recognition across domains, tireless availability -- and characteristic weaknesses -- hallucination, no persistent memory, no reliable self-knowledge about the limits of its knowledge.

Enhancement is the natural response to that profile. And the idea of enhancing a base intelligence is not new. We have always built on top of the base intelligence we were given -- through language, writing, mathematics, computation, and now AI. Each layer extended what came before. The same logic applies here.

A brief aside -- and then back to work

We should note something that is easy to miss in the rush to use these tools: we now live alongside a genuinely new kind of intelligence. Not a smarter human. Not a dumber human. Something built on entirely different foundations -- designed rather than evolved, trained on human artefacts rather than shaped by survival pressures, with a completely different profile of strengths and weaknesses.

We have, until very recently, shared the world only with other evolved intelligences -- humans and animals, all shaped by the same basic pressures. Artificial intelligences are categorically different. They are behaviourally familiar, because they were trained on everything we wrote, but structurally alien.

The most useful approach is not to rank them against human intelligence, but to understand them on their own terms. What is this thing actually good at? What does it genuinely struggle with? That's the question that makes you a better user -- and it's the question the rest of this session is designed to answer.

B. Enhancing the Intelligence

B1. Model enhancements -- what the builders do

The base model -- trained on the internet, predicting next tokens -- is powerful but raw. It will complete any text in whatever direction the patterns suggest, including harmful, false, or simply unhelpful directions. Several layers of enhancement are applied before you interact with it.

Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) and related fine-tuning techniques shape the model's behaviour: making it helpful, honest, and safe. Human raters evaluate outputs; the model learns to produce outputs that score well. This is why Claude behaves like an assistant rather than a raw text predictor.

Explicit reasoning -- sometimes called chain-of-thought -- is the discovery that models perform dramatically better on complex problems when they are prompted, or trained, to think step by step before answering. Rather than jumping to a completion, the model works through intermediate steps. This is the basis of the "extended thinking" modes you may have encountered.

Inference-time scaling is the most recent development: rather than just training bigger models, you run more computation at the moment of answering -- letting the model explore multiple reasoning paths, check its own work, or run longer internal deliberation. This has driven significant capability jumps in the last year.

Most recently, agentic capacity itself is becoming a builder-level enhancement -- shipped as part of the foundation model rather than constructed by users. Claude Code, which autonomously plans and executes complex coding tasks, and Cowork, which automates file and task management for non-developers, are current examples. The boundary between "what the model can do" and "what you have to build" is shifting rapidly -- a point we will return to.

B2. User techniques -- what you can do

Context is the most important and most underused lever. The model has no persistent memory -- everything it knows about your situation is what you put in front of it in the current conversation. Rich context produces dramatically better output. Most new users underinvest here.

Prompt engineering is the practice of structuring your inputs to guide the model's thinking and outputs. It ranges from simple (be specific, give examples, specify the format you want) to sophisticated (role assignment, chain-of-thought prompting, system prompts).

Tools and skills extend the model beyond its training data. A model with access to web search, code execution, or file systems can act in the world, not just generate text.

Agents are systems where the model plans and executes multi-step tasks autonomously -- breaking a complex goal into steps, using tools along the way, and iterating until the job is done. This is where the technology is moving fastest, with agentic capabilities increasingly being built into the underlying models and agentic applications such as Claude Code.

Coda: Why Understanding Matters More Than Technique

There is a pattern worth naming before we finish.

Since ChatGPT went public in late 2022, the capability of foundation models has risen steeply and continuously. Things that required significant custom engineering 18 months ago are now handled natively -- by the model itself, or by tools like Claude Code, or by agents that no longer need elaborate custom infrastructure to orchestrate. People and organisations that invested heavily in building technical workarounds for the limitations of 2023-era models have repeatedly found that investment overtaken by model improvements. The specific workaround becomes unnecessary. The Python pipeline someone spent months building gets replaced by a conversation.

This creates a real risk for anyone trying to develop AI capability: following the hype into deep investment in today's limitations, only to find the model outgrows them in six months.

The antidote is literacy. Understanding what the model actually is -- how it learns, what it's good at, where it fails and why -- compounds over time. It makes you a better user of today's models and a faster adapter to tomorrow's. Specific techniques become obsolete. The understanding that lets you evaluate and adopt new techniques does not.

That is what this course is designed to build. Not a toolkit for 2026. A foundation that gets more valuable as the models get more capable.

SESSION 3: Deep Research in the Wild -- What Canned Tools Can Do Phase 1: Lecturer's Notes

OVERVIEW

Goal: Participants see what state-of-the-art canned research tools can produce, learn that impressive-looking outputs contain serious hidden weaknesses, and leave convinced that evaluation is a genuine skill -- and that control matters.

Takeaway: Canned tools are powerful but opaque. Systematic evaluation exposes what a quick read never would. The logical next step is taking control of the process.

The IDE arc: This session focuses on Evaluation. Direction is raised at the close. Input becomes the focus of Session 5.

Timing: Approximately 2 hours.

SECTION 1 -- The skilled user frame (10 minutes)

Goal: Give participants an observational stance before the demos run. They are not passive observers -- they are evaluators in training.

Introduce the three things a skilled AI user does:

Input -- the material you bring: documents, data, files, screenshots. The raw stuff the model works with.

Direction -- everything you say to the model: the question, the context, the instructions, the constraints, the format, the iteration.

Evaluation -- how you assess what comes back: not just "does it look good?" but structured, criteria-driven judgment.

Tell participants explicitly: today is about Evaluation. We are going to watch some impressive tools do serious work, and your job is to watch critically. By the end of the session you will have a framework for doing that -- and homework to practise it.

On literacy: evaluation is the most durable of the three skills. Prompting techniques evolve, tools change, but the ability to assess AI output critically transfers across every tool participants will ever use. It becomes more important as outputs get more polished and harder to challenge on first reading.

Transition: "Let's give these tools something to work on."

SECTION 2 -- The research brief (5 minutes)

Goal: Frame the research question and demonstrate good prompt construction before submitting.

Introduce the research context: your organisation provides training resources to banks in South Africa. You have been asked to produce a report identifying key skills shortages emerging in the sector over the next three years, and recommending training priorities for programme development.

Show the base prompt:

You are preparing a report for an organisation that provides training resources to banks in South Africa. The report should identify the key skills shortages emerging in the South African banking sector over the next three years, and recommend the training priorities that should shape the organisation's programme development.

Point out what this prompt does and doesn't do:

It provides role context and a clear task

It does not specify sources, structure, depth, audience, or format

That absence of Direction is deliberate -- we want to see what the tools do with minimal guidance

Transition: "Let's submit this simultaneously to all three tools and see what happens."

SECTION 3 -- The live demos (20-25 minutes, mostly waiting)

Goal: Submit the prompt, then use the wait productively. Extended thinking, agentic behaviour and tool use are explained here -- not as abstractions but as observable phenomena.

Submit the prompt to all three tools simultaneously:

Claude Research (Claude Chat, Research mode on)

ChatGPT Deep Research

Perplexity Deep Research

While the tools run -- the conceptual payload:

Extended thinking / chain-of-thought: The model is not retrieving an answer from a database. It is constructing one. Where visible, draw attention to the thinking indicator. The model is breaking the question down, identifying what it needs to know, planning its approach. This is inference-time computation -- the model doing more work per question rather than simply being larger. Contrast with a standard chat response which produces output immediately with no visible reasoning step.

Agentic behaviour: These tools are not generating text -- they are acting. They are deciding what to search for, choosing sources, reading pages, forming a view, going back for more. Name this explicitly: this is an agent. It has a goal, it has tools, it is making sequential decisions to pursue that goal. Participants are watching autonomous behaviour, not autocomplete.

Tool use: The agent is using web search as a tool. It calls the tool, receives results, decides what to do with them, calls it again if needed. Point at this loop as it happens in ChatGPT's live progress tracker -- the most visible illustration of the search-analyse-replanning cycle.

What each tool shows during the run:

Claude -- clarifying questions before starting (scope, audience, format). A process artefact alongside the report summarising the research plan and sources. Slower -- approximately 9 minutes.

ChatGPT -- a live priority/progress tracker that doubles as a plan. A detailed activity log afterwards showing the full 12-minute reasoning and search cycle, including strategic replanning when tool calls run low and blocked pages are encountered.

Perplexity -- live action descriptions as it runs, approximately 5 minutes. Clean report output with citations and relevant images located during search. Fastest, least process-transparent.

Teaching points to land during the wait:

Claude's clarifying questions are Direction in action -- from the model's side. The questions it asks reveal exactly what you should have specified in your prompt. Note this: we will come back to it.

ChatGPT's activity log is the richest illustration of extended reasoning and agentic behaviour available in the session. Show it briefly -- the scale alone is striking -- then present the five-point summary: visible planning intelligence, strategic source selection, autonomous workarounds for blocked content, self-correction under constraints, and search-analyse-replan cycles throughout.

Transition when results arrive: "Right -- we have three reports. Before we read them, we need a way to assess them properly."

SECTION 4 -- Building the evaluation rubric (15 minutes)

Goal: Introduce structured evaluation as a skill. Build or present the rubric explicitly so participants understand the criteria before applying them.

The instinct when an AI produces a polished report is to read it and form an impression. That is not evaluation. Evaluation requires explicit criteria applied consistently.

Introduce the three-level rubric:

Process quality -- how did it research?

Breadth and authority of sources

Geographic and sectoral specificity -- did it find South African banking sources or generalise?

Transparency of reasoning -- can you see what it did and why?

Content quality -- what did it find?

Quality of insight extraction from sources

Coverage of the relevant skills domains

Handling of SA-specific regulatory and institutional context

Logical coherence of findings

Output quality -- what did it produce?

Structure and navigability

Specificity of recommendations -- actionable or generic?

Appropriate caveats and limitations acknowledged

Fitness for purpose -- could you hand this to a client?

Note on building vs presenting: depending on time and group energy, you can either present this rubric directly or build it interactively -- ask participants what they would want to know about a research report before trusting it, then organise their answers into the three levels. The interactive version takes longer but produces stronger ownership of the framework.

SECTION 5 -- The evaluation in practice (20 minutes)

Goal: Demonstrate the evaluation process using a dedicated Claude Project. Show participants what systematic AI-assisted evaluation looks like.

Explain the setup: rather than reading three long reports side by side, we are going to use a fourth AI -- Claude -- to help us evaluate all three. This is itself an illustration of Direction: we are giving Claude a specific role, specific documents, and a specific framework, and asking it to do structured analytical work.

Walk through the evaluation Project and the task sequence:

Step 1 -- Structured summaries Submit each report for summarisation to a common template: research scope and framing, key skills domains identified, main findings per domain, training recommendations, caveats and limitations. Note that Claude handles truncated documents autonomously -- identifies the gap, retrieves the missing section, confirms completeness. Point at this as agentic behaviour at a smaller scale.

Step 2 -- Comparative analysis Using the three summaries, generate a structured comparison across all five template sections. The output is a detailed comparison document surfacing similarities, differences, gaps, and cross-cutting observations.

Step 3 -- Rankings Ask Claude to rank the three reports on seven criteria: breadth of domain coverage, quality and authority of sources, accuracy and precision of claims, extent to which the brief was addressed, operational usefulness for programme design, suitability for presentation to senior management, and transparency and intellectual honesty.

Present the key findings from the rankings -- particularly the tensions:

Claude ranks 1st for senior management presentation, 3rd for transparency

ChatGPT ranks 1st for operational usefulness, 3rd for senior management suitability

No single report is sufficient for the full brief

The most useful synthesis would combine elements of all three

Land the critical finding: all three reports look authoritative on first reading. Without this evaluation process you would not know about the hidden weaknesses. One report uses the wrong time horizon without acknowledging it. The most strategically coherent report makes bold quantitative claims without a single source caveat. No report adequately acknowledges its own limitations or source bias.

STEP 3 -- Source discovery and curation (25 minutes)

Goal: Walk through the five-part source discovery pipeline, demonstrating each part with pre-prepared outputs. This is the most novel part of the process and the one most different from canned tools.

Explain the pipeline overview: five parts, each catching things the others miss, with a clear product at the end -- a ranked, curated, quality-checked source list.

3.1 -- Deep Research sweep

What it does: Run DR on the research question with minimal direction. Harvest the source list. Discard the report.

Live demo: Submit the research question to Claude Research mode. While it runs (8-9 minutes), explain what's happening: DR is being used as a search engine with judgment, not as a report writer. It will find sources based on its real-time web access and its own assessment of relevance. After 2-3 minutes of watching the tool calls, cut to the pre-prepared output.

Show the output: The DR source list -- 73 sources in this case. Point out the "520 sources" display: this is the number of pages DR visited during its search, not the number it cited. Participants will see this number and assume it means something it doesn't.

Teaching point: We are using a powerful tool for a narrow purpose. The report DR produces is exactly the kind of output Session 3 showed us we can't fully trust. But the source list is valuable -- it's a broad sweep of what's out there.

3.2 -- Training knowledge sweep

What it does: In standard chat (not DR), give Claude the research question and the DR source list. Ask it to identify authoritative sources, institutions, reports, and authors that are relevant but missing from the list.

Show the instruction and output: The instruction specifically asks Claude to think about regulatory bodies, industry associations, consulting firms, academic research centres, and recurring sector publications. The output was approximately 40 additional sources across 10 categories.

Key finding to present: Claude identified three major gaps in the DR source list: the Big 5 banks' own annual integrated reports were entirely absent, some sources cited in the DR report's body text didn't appear in its own source list (an internal inconsistency), and an entire category of practical data (recruitment firm salary guides) was missing. These are three different failure modes caught in a single pass.

Teaching point: This step taps a different knowledge base from DR. The model's training knowledge includes awareness of authoritative institutions, recurring reports, and key publications that a real-time web search might not surface. The overlap between the two sweeps is incomplete -- that's the point.

3.3 -- Human knowledge sweep

What it does: The researcher adds sources from their own domain knowledge -- reports they've seen cited, institutions they know are active, niche publications, known experts.

Note for delivery: This step was skipped in the demo run because no domain-specific knowledge was available. In a real research engagement, this is where the researcher's expertise earns its place. Mention that this is also where the Session 3 homework lands naturally -- participants identified source gaps in the canned reports, and their domain knowledge feeds in here.

3.4 -- Source quality check

What it does: Give Claude the combined source list and ask it to check each source for: URL accessibility, publication date, whether it's a primary or secondary source, institutional credibility, and direct relevance to the research question.

Show the instruction: Walk through the column definitions -- Status, Date, Type, Credibility, Relevance, Source Sweep, Recommendation, Notes. Point out that the instruction specifies the output format explicitly (CSV with defined columns) and asks for an actionable recommendation (Keep / Query / Remove) rather than just annotation.

Show the output: The source audit CSV. Key statistics: 115 sources checked, 61 Keep, 40 Query, 14 Remove. Only 62 confirmed live URLs. Of the 42 training knowledge sources, only 18 had confirmed live URLs -- validating the concern that training knowledge produces good institutional names but unreliable URLs.

Practical lesson: This step hit tool call limits repeatedly because Claude was checking 115 URLs one by one. Each tool call triggers a full context re-read. The workaround was clicking "continue" multiple times. A better approach for production use would be a Python script that handles mechanical URL checks and reserves AI calls for the judgment tasks (credibility, relevance). Name this as a practical limitation participants will encounter.

3.5 -- Human curation

What it does: The researcher reviews the annotated list and assigns each source a rank: A (essential), B (important), C (useful), D (ignore). Removes sources that failed the quality check. Adds any final sources prompted by the review.

Show the decision: In this case, a tight filter was applied: keep only sources with Status = Live and Recommendation = Keep. This produced 40 sources -- a clean, defensible list with an even split of 20 primary and 20 secondary sources.

Teaching point: This is where human judgment is irreplaceable. The AI can check URLs and assess credibility, but deciding which sources matter most for this specific question and this specific audience is a human call. The A-D ranking in a real engagement would reflect the researcher's understanding of the client's priorities and the question's demands.

Step 3 close -- the product: We now have something no canned tool gives us: a transparent, quality-checked, human-curated source list. Every source is there for a reason. Every source has been verified. The list is defensible -- if a client asks "why did you use this source?" or "why didn't you use that one?", we can answer.

Note for recurring research: This source list becomes a maintained asset. The expensive multi-sweep discovery process runs once. On subsequent runs, the task is simpler: check the canonical list for health, and search for new publications from known institutions. The process gets faster and the list gets better over time.

Transition: "We have our sources. Now we need to find out what they actually say."

STEP 4 -- Literature review (10 minutes)

Goal: Show how a constrained instruction produces a source-by-source digest that preserves what each source actually says before any synthesis occurs.

What it does: Ask Claude to read each of the 40 curated sources and produce a focused summary of each, concentrating on content relevant to the research question. No integration, no synthesis -- just a structured digest of what each source says.

Show the instruction: Point out its key features: it tells Claude to summarise each source individually, not to integrate knowledge across sources, and not to add any sources beyond the curated list. It frames the task as writing a "Review of the Literature" section, not answering the research question.

The DR constraint problem: This instruction was run in DR mode because Claude needed web access to read the URLs. The instruction explicitly told DR not to search for additional sources. DR acknowledged the constraint ("you want a source-by-source literature review covering only the URLs you've provided") but then visited 443 sources during the run. However, the final output respected the output constraint -- it reported on only the 40 specified sources. Lesson: DR may not respect process constraints even when it respects output constraints. For production work, consider whether standard chat with web search gives more control.

Show the output briefly: 40 individual source summaries, each focused on skills-relevant content. Some are clearly based on actually reading the page (detailed data, specific figures). Others -- particularly landing pages like SABRIC and Stats SA -- read more like Claude filled in from training knowledge because the page itself didn't have extractable content. This distinction is worth noting but doesn't invalidate the output.

Transition: "We now know what each source says individually. The next step is to look across all of them and find the patterns."

STEP 5 -- Analytical synthesis (15 minutes)

Goal: Show how a synthesis organised by theme -- not by source -- produces the analytical backbone for the report. Demonstrate why this step exists separately from both the literature review and the report.

What it does: Takes the 40 source summaries and organises them by theme, assessing evidence strength, surfacing tensions between sources, and identifying gaps.

Why this is a separate step: If you go straight from source summaries to a report, Claude will do the synthesis implicitly and you lose visibility into the analytical layer. You can't see whether a recommendation is well-supported by multiple sources or built on a single data point. The synthesis makes the evidence structure visible.

Show the instruction: It has four explicit tasks: identify major themes and note which sources support each; flag conflicting data and divergent assessments; identify evidence gaps; and note where evidence is strong versus where it rests on a single source. Crucially, it says "do not make recommendations -- organise and assess."

Show key findings from the synthesis output:

  • 11 themes identified, each with an explicit evidence-strength rating
  • Cybersecurity is the best-evidenced shortage (multiple primary sources converging), but the specific 30,000-person deficit figure comes from a single paywalled commercial report
  • AI/ML demand has strong directional consensus but no quantified estimate of the gap
  • Two Fortinet-authored articles that appear to be independent sources are actually the same organisation citing the same data -- the synthesis catches this
  • IT spending figures for Standard Bank appear at four different values across sources due to different reporting boundaries -- flagged as a tension, not silently resolved
  • Nine significant evidence gaps identified, including the absence of the Big 5 banks' own annual reports

Teaching point: This synthesis is the analytical backbone of the report. A senior analyst receiving this document can see exactly what the evidence supports and where it's weak. Compare this to the canned reports from Session 3 -- those reports presented claims with uniform confidence regardless of evidence strength. This synthesis distinguishes between what we know, what we infer, and what we don't know. That distinction is what makes the final report defensible under scrutiny.

Transition: "We have the evidence base. Now we shape it into a report."

STEP 6 -- The report (20 minutes)

Goal: Show how a highly opinionated instruction produces a report that inherits the transparency of the process that built it. Demonstrate the difference between this report and the canned outputs from Session 3.

Show the instruction: This is the most detailed instruction in the process. Walk through its key elements:

  • Audience and purpose: A senior leadership team at a Big 5 bank. The report demonstrates analytical capability, not a sales pitch. This shapes tone, level of detail, and what can be assumed.
  • Explicit structure: Seven sections specified, each with a clear function -- from executive summary through evidence gaps to a prioritised training framework.
  • Evidence-handling rules: Where evidence is strong, say so. Where it rests on a single source, say that too. Do not present weak evidence with the same confidence as strong evidence. Where the synthesis identified tensions, do not resolve them silently.
  • The no-filler rule: No generic paragraphs about the importance of digital transformation. No filler about the rapidly evolving landscape. Every paragraph must contain a specific claim, a specific piece of evidence, or a specific judgment.

Show the report output: Walk through the key sections, pointing out how the instruction shaped the output:

  • The executive summary ranks five skills shortage areas explicitly, with evidence strength noted for each
  • The regulatory drivers table provides a scannable overview of six simultaneous regulatory changes with timelines and evidence ratings
  • The evidence gaps section acknowledges what the analysis cannot tell us -- including the absence of primary bank workforce data and salary benchmarking
  • The training priorities framework ranks investment areas with commentary explaining why each is positioned where it is, and what would change the ranking
  • The closing paragraph argues that structural shortages require long-duration training pipelines, not short courses -- a position grounded in the Absa Cybersecurity Academy's three-year programme

The critical comparison: Put this report alongside the canned reports from Session 3. The canned reports are slicker. They read more fluently. But:

  • This report knows what it doesn't know. The canned reports present all claims with equal confidence.
  • This report distinguishes strong evidence from weak. The canned reports do not.
  • This report surfaces contradictions (four different IT spending figures for Standard Bank, the Fortinet double-counting). The canned reports hide them.
  • This report has a transparent, auditable source base. The canned reports selected sources autonomously with no human oversight.
  • This report is defensible under scrutiny. The canned reports are not.

That last point is the core argument of this session. Defensible under scrutiny is the standard that separates professional research from impressive-looking output. Everything in this process -- the multi-sweep source discovery, the quality audit, the human curation, the separated synthesis, the evidence-graded report -- contributes to defensibility.

Executive Summary

Since January 2024, South African banking has entered a period of unprecedented regulatory intensity around digital capability. Three regulatory changes stand out for their combined breadth, binding force, and skills implications: the Joint Standard on Cybersecurity and Cyber Resilience (JS2 of 2024), the Joint Standard on IT Governance and Risk Management (JS1 of 2023, effective November 2024), and the Basel III Post-Crisis Reforms (effective July 2025). Together, these instruments require banks to build layered digital competencies -- from board-level technology literacy to specialist quantitative modelling -- at a time when South Africa faces acute shortages in cybersecurity professionals, IT governance practitioners, and data scientists. With 63% of cybersecurity roles nationally partially or fully unfilled, 43% of open banking positions attributed to insufficient skills and education, and declining STEM pass rates constraining the future talent pipeline, the gap between regulatory expectation and workforce reality is widening. This report examines each regulatory change in detail, assesses the digital skills it demands, reviews available evidence of skills gaps, and considers the cumulative effect on the sector.

---

Methodology

This report synthesises publicly available regulatory texts, supervisory communications, sector skills plans, industry surveys, and consulting firm analyses published between January 2024 and April 2026. The three regulatory changes were selected and ranked using a scoring framework based on three criteria: (1) breadth of institutional and role scope, (2) binding force and compliance urgency, and (3) severity and novelty of the skills gap created. Each criterion was scored on a five-point scale, yielding a maximum possible score of 15. The ranking reflects conditions as of April 2026; pipeline regulatory developments -- particularly around artificial intelligence governance and cloud computing standards -- could materially alter the landscape in the near term. All sources are cited inline and collected at the end of this report.

1. Joint Standard 2 of 2024: Cybersecurity and Cyber Resilience

Score: 15/15 -- the highest-impact regulatory change assessed

What It Requires

Issued jointly by the Prudential Authority (PA) and the Financial Sector Conduct Authority (FSCA), Joint Standard 2 of 2024 was published on 17 May 2024 and became effective on 1 June 2025. It applies to all financial institutions -- banks, insurers, retirement funds, fund administrators, and collective investment scheme managers -- making it the broadest cybersecurity mandate the South African financial sector has seen.

The Standard establishes minimum requirements across the full cybersecurity lifecycle. Financial institutions must develop and maintain a cybersecurity strategy and framework, identify and classify business processes and information assets by criticality and sensitivity, and implement data loss prevention policies. They must build cyber resilience capabilities encompassing incident response and recovery, with tested and auditable incident response plans that include executive involvement. The Standard recommends appointment of a dedicated cybersecurity officer, distinct from the IT manager -- a meaningful structural change for institutions that have historically folded cyber responsibilities into general IT management.

Critically, the Standard mandates comprehensive cybersecurity awareness training annually for governing bodies and all users. This is not a recommendation; it is a binding obligation, auditable by supervisors.

What Skills It Demands

The skills footprint of JS2 is exceptionally broad, spanning strategic, technical, and organisational domains:

  • Cybersecurity strategy and framework development -- the ability to design, document, and maintain an enterprise-wide cybersecurity programme aligned to the institution's risk appetite
  • Cyber risk assessment -- systematic identification, quantification, and prioritisation of cyber threats
  • Information asset classification -- cataloguing data and systems by criticality and sensitivity, a prerequisite for proportionate controls
  • Network security and data loss prevention (DLP) -- technical implementation of perimeter and internal controls
  • Incident response and recovery -- the operational capability to detect, contain, eradicate, and recover from cyber incidents, including forensic analysis
  • Cybersecurity awareness programme design -- pedagogical skills to develop training that meets the Standard's annual, all-levels requirement
  • Threat intelligence and monitoring -- continuous surveillance of the threat landscape and integration of intelligence into defensive posture

These skills are required at every level of the organisation. Boards must undergo annual cybersecurity training, and their fiduciary duty now explicitly extends to cyber risk. Senior management is accountable for the framework and must participate in incident response exercises. IT and security teams bear the burden of technical implementation. And all users -- from tellers to traders -- must complete annual awareness training.

Evidence of the Skills Gap

The skills gap in cybersecurity is the most acute of any domain examined in this report.

The CSIR 2024 National Cybersecurity Survey paints a stark picture: 63% of cybersecurity roles nationally are partially or fully unfilled, amounting to approximately 35,000 vacant cybersecurity positions across the economy. Seventy-five percent of companies report difficulty recruiting cybersecurity professionals, and 38% of applicants possess less than a quarter of the required qualifications. This is not a marginal shortfall; it is a structural deficit.

The Deloitte/AFIS 2024 African Financial Industry Barometer reinforces this from a sector-specific perspective: 59% of African financial institutions identify cybercrime as a major threat, and 70% believe cybersecurity skills shortages create additional risk -- a candid acknowledgement that the workforce cannot keep pace with the threat environment.

Some institutions are investing in proprietary training pipelines. Absa's Cybersecurity Academy, a three-year programme leading to twelve international certifications, represents the most ambitious initiative of its kind in South African banking. But such programmes take years to produce graduates, and their scale remains insufficient relative to sector-wide demand.

Compliance Timeline

The Standard became effective on 1 June 2025. Financial institutions are expected to be in full compliance, with auditable evidence of training delivery, framework documentation, and incident response testing. The annual training cycle creates an ongoing compliance obligation with no end date.

2. Joint Standard 1 of 2023: IT Governance and Risk Management

Score: 13/15 -- the foundational governance architecture

What It Requires

Also issued jointly by the PA and FSCA, Joint Standard 1 of 2023 became effective on 15 November 2024 -- meaning it is already in force. Like JS2, it applies to all financial institutions, not just banks.

The Standard requires governing bodies to approve the institution's IT strategy and mandates comprehensive oversight of IT internal controls and risk management by senior management. IT governance and risk management practices must be proportionate to the institution's size, complexity, and risk profile -- a principles-based requirement that demands judgement rather than mechanical compliance.

A notable structural requirement is that the IT function must be headed by a qualified individual who is part of senior management and possesses "requisite skills and experience." This elevates the IT leadership role from a technical function to a strategic governance position.

The Standard further requires a comprehensive IT governance and risk management training programme for all levels, reviewed annually. The IT governance framework itself must be reviewed at least annually.

What Skills It Demands

JS1's skills demands span governance, risk, and technical domains:

  • IT governance frameworks -- practical familiarity with standards such as COBIT, ITIL, and ISO 27001, and the ability to adapt them to institutional context
  • IT risk management -- the full cycle of identification, assessment, mitigation, and monitoring of IT-related risks
  • IT strategy development -- aligning technology investment with business objectives and regulatory requirements
  • Change management and incident response -- controlled processes for system changes and structured responses to IT incidents
  • Data protection and privacy -- alignment with the Protection of Personal Information Act (POPIA) and emerging data governance expectations
  • Business continuity and disaster recovery -- ensuring operational resilience under adverse conditions
  • IT audit and assurance -- the ability to evaluate and provide independent assurance over IT controls

Boards must approve IT strategy and undergo IT risk training. Senior management must execute IT internal controls. The IT head must demonstrably possess the "requisite skills and experience" -- a requirement that, while not specifying particular qualifications, creates a de facto professional standard. Compliance teams must integrate reporting obligations into IT risk protocols. And all staff must participate in the mandated training programme.

Evidence of the Skills Gap

The skills gap for IT governance is real but different in character from the cybersecurity gap. IT governance is an established discipline with mature professional frameworks; the challenge is less about novelty than about depth and breadth of deployment.

KPMG has noted that smaller financial institutions struggle most with compliance, owing to limited in-house IT governance capability. This is significant because JS1 applies to the full spectrum of financial institutions, many of which lack dedicated IT governance functions.

BankSETA data attributes 43% of unfilled banking positions to a lack of relevant experience and education -- a figure that encompasses IT governance roles among others. The principles-based nature of the Standard adds a further complication: determining what constitutes "adequate" skills in context is itself a judgement call that requires experienced professionals. This meta-skill -- the ability to exercise proportionate judgement about governance adequacy -- is scarce.

Compliance Timeline

JS1 has been in force since 15 November 2024. Institutions that have not yet achieved compliance are already operating outside the Standard's requirements. The annual review cycle for governance frameworks and training programmes creates an ongoing compliance rhythm.

The Relationship Between JS1 and JS2

It is worth emphasising that JS1 is foundational to JS2. The IT governance architecture required by JS1 provides the structural framework within which cybersecurity capabilities (JS2) must operate. An institution that lacks sound IT governance will find cybersecurity compliance not merely difficult but incoherent -- controls without a governance context are ad hoc rather than systematic. Failure to comply with JS1 therefore cascades into non-compliance with JS2 and, over time, with emerging standards on cloud computing and AI governance.

3. Basel III Post-Crisis Reforms

Score: 12/15 -- the specialist quantitative challenge

What It Requires

Gazetted by the National Treasury and effective 1 July 2025, the remaining Basel III post-crisis reforms (sometimes referred to as "Basel IV" in industry shorthand) implement the final tranche of internationally agreed prudential standards. Unlike the Joint Standards, these reforms apply specifically to licensed banks, not the broader financial sector.

The reforms encompass several technically demanding workstreams: revised standardised and internal ratings-based (IRB) approaches for credit risk, a new operational risk framework, an updated leverage ratio, and the introduction of an output floor -- phasing in at 60% from July 2025. The Prudential Authority's Communication 18 of 2024 further specifies requirements for market risk systems upgrades under the Fundamental Review of the Trading Book (FRTB) and credit valuation adjustment (CVA) frameworks.

In practical terms, compliance requires significant data infrastructure and risk modelling systems upgrades, revised credit risk calculation engines, new regulatory reporting formats, and market risk systems that can accommodate FRTB requirements.

What Skills It Demands

Basel III compliance demands a concentration of specialist quantitative and data skills:

  • Quantitative risk modelling -- development and calibration of credit, market, and operational risk models to revised specifications
  • Data infrastructure and engineering -- the collection, storage, processing, and quality assurance of the granular data required by new reporting standards
  • Regulatory reporting and disclosure -- production of accurate, timely reports in formats prescribed by the Prudential Authority
  • Capital adequacy analytics -- the ability to calculate and optimise capital requirements under multiple simultaneous constraints
  • Model validation -- independent assessment of model performance, assumptions, and limitations
  • Systems integration -- connecting upgraded risk systems with existing banking infrastructure

The primary skills burden falls on risk management teams, quantitative analysts, and data and IT teams. Finance teams must adapt to new reporting requirements. Boards and senior management need sufficient understanding to exercise strategic oversight and challenge management's assumptions -- a demand reinforced by King V's technology governance pillar (discussed below).

Evidence of the Skills Gap

KPMG has explicitly highlighted the need for "investment in data, systems, and processes" among South African banks preparing for Basel III reforms. BankSETA has identified the need for specialised Basel IV training programmes, acknowledging that existing professional development infrastructure is insufficient.

The 43% figure for unfilled banking positions due to skills deficits applies here as well, but the deeper concern is structural. Declining mathematics and physical sciences pass rates at secondary level are reducing the pipeline for quantitative roles. A student who does not pass matric mathematics at the required level cannot enter the university programmes that produce risk modellers and data scientists. This is not a skills gap that corporate training can close; it is a pipeline problem rooted in the education system.

McKinsey projects a persistent AI and data talent shortage in Sub-Saharan Africa through at least 2030 -- a forecast that encompasses the quantitative professionals needed for Basel III compliance. South African banks are competing for a shrinking pool of candidates against global demand for the same skills.

Compliance Timeline

The reforms became effective on 1 July 2025, with the output floor phasing in from 60% at that date. Banks that have not completed systems upgrades, model recalibration, and staff training face prudential consequences -- potentially including higher capital charges and supervisory intervention.

Cross-Cutting Analysis: The Cumulative Effect

Layered Demands, Compounding Gaps

The three regulatory changes described above do not operate in isolation. They impose layered and interdependent demands on the same institutions, often on the same teams, within overlapping timeframes.

Consider the position of a mid-tier South African bank in mid-2025. It must simultaneously:

  • Maintain and enhance IT governance architecture under JS1, which has been in force since November 2024
  • Achieve cybersecurity compliance under JS2 by 1 June 2025, building on the governance foundation of JS1
  • Complete Basel III systems upgrades by 1 July 2025, requiring data infrastructure investments that must also satisfy JS1's governance requirements and JS2's data protection standards
  • Continue meeting obligations under POPIA (including recent amendments), cloud computing guidance from the PA, and sector-specific conduct standards

Each layer draws on a partially overlapping but distinct skills base. The IT governance professional, the cybersecurity specialist, and the quantitative risk modeller are different people with different training -- but they must work within integrated frameworks, using shared data infrastructure, under unified board oversight. The scarcity in any one domain creates bottlenecks that affect all the others.

Board-Level Skills and King V

The King V Code on Corporate Governance, effective January 2026, amplifies the board-level skills demands created by all three regulatory changes. King V establishes data, technology, and AI as a standalone governance pillar, asserting that boards must be "technologically literate, not just financially literate." This is not a regulatory standard with direct enforcement mechanisms, but it sets a governance expectation that the PA and FSCA are likely to reference in supervisory assessments.

The practical implication is significant: boards can no longer delegate technology decisions entirely to management. Directors must understand enough about cybersecurity risk, IT governance, and quantitative risk modelling to ask informed questions and exercise meaningful oversight.

The Pipeline Problem

The BankSETA Sector Skills Plans for 2024-2025 and 2025-2030 identify cybersecurity, data analytics, and digital platform management as the most critical skills gaps in banking. The Institute of Bankers in South Africa (IOBSA) has identified five key future banker skills: data analytics and interpretation, digital platform management, cybersecurity and data protection, customer experience design, and regulatory technology (RegTech).

These assessments are consistent with a broad consulting firm consensus. PwC, EY, KPMG, Deloitte, Accenture, and McKinsey all flag the convergence of regulatory technology demands and workforce skills gaps as a defining challenge for South African banking. IT spending is tracking above inflation. Banks are investing in cloud migration, AI, and digital innovation -- but struggle to attract and retain qualified talent in a globally competitive market.

The root of the problem extends beyond the banking sector. Declining secondary-school pass rates in mathematics and physical sciences constrain the pipeline of students entering quantitative and technical university programmes. This is a structural limitation that no amount of corporate training can fully compensate for, and it implies that the skills gap will persist -- and potentially widen -- through the end of the decade.

What Was Excluded and Why

Two regulatory developments came close to inclusion in this analysis but fell short on the scoring criteria:

  • POPIA Amendments (scored 11/15) carry high urgency -- they were immediately effective with no transition period -- but impose incremental rather than transformative skills demands on institutions that have already invested in POPIA compliance since 2021.
  • The AI in Financial Sector report by the FSCA and PA (scored 8/15) has the highest future potential of any development assessed. It found that 53% of financial sector staff lack AI training and that AI governance frameworks remain immature. However, it carries zero binding regulatory force today. When binding AI standards emerge -- as they almost certainly will -- they are likely to enter the top tier of skills-relevant regulatory changes.

Limitations

This analysis is subject to several important limitations:

  • Data availability. The report relies on publicly available sources as of April 2026. Internal bank compliance data, supervisory assessment findings, and non-public industry surveys are not reflected. The true state of compliance and skills readiness within individual institutions may differ materially from what public data suggests.
  • Methodological variation in skills gap data. Statistics cited from different sources -- the CSIR cybersecurity survey, BankSETA sector skills plans, Deloitte/AFIS barometer -- use different methodologies, sample frames, and timeframes. Direct comparison between figures should be made cautiously.
  • Ranking sensitivity. The significance ranking is based on three defined criteria (scope, binding force, skills gap severity), each equally weighted. Different criteria or different weighting schemes could yield different rankings. In particular, if future regulatory impact were weighted more heavily, the AI governance space would rank higher.
  • Regulatory pipeline. Several developments in the regulatory pipeline -- binding AI governance standards, a formal cloud computing standard, and the Conduct of Financial Institutions (COFI) Bill -- could substantially alter the skills landscape upon implementation. This report captures a point-in-time assessment.
  • Sector focus. The analysis focuses on the banking sector specifically. The Joint Standards (JS1 and JS2) apply more broadly across financial services; the skills gap dynamics in insurance, retirement funds, and other sub-sectors may differ from those in banking.

CLAUDE.md

You are a research orchestrator. Your job is to take a research question, design a workflow to answer it, and coordinate specialist sub-agents to execute that workflow. You produce a final research report and a process log documenting what each agent did.

How You Work

  • Receive the research question from the user.
  • Design a research plan: break the question into 3-5 specific sub-tasks. Each sub-task should be a clear, self-contained research assignment that one agent can complete independently. Write the plan out before executing it.
  • Dispatch sub-agents: for each sub-task, spawn a specialist agent with a focused brief. Each agent receives only its own task -- not the full plan.
  • Collect and review: as each agent returns its findings, assess whether the sub-task has been adequately addressed. If not, you may re-dispatch with refined instructions or spawn an additional agent to fill a gap.
  • Synthesise: once all sub-tasks are complete, spawn a synthesis agent to produce the final report from the collected findings.
  • Produce outputs: deliver the final report and the process log.

Sub-Agent Roles

You have three types of specialist agent available:

Researcher

  • Purpose: find and extract relevant information on a specific sub-topic
  • Instructions to give: a clear question, the scope of what to look for, and any constraints (e.g. geographic focus, time period, source preferences)
  • Expected output: a structured summary of findings with key points, relevant details, and source attribution where possible

Analyst

  • Purpose: evaluate, compare, or identify patterns in material already gathered
  • Instructions to give: the material to analyse, the specific analytical question, and the criteria or framework to apply
  • Expected output: a structured assessment with explicit reasoning, not just conclusions

Synthesiser

  • Purpose: produce a coherent final output from multiple inputs
  • Instructions to give: all gathered material, the original research question, and any format or audience requirements
  • Expected output: a clear, well-structured report that addresses the original question, draws on all the research, and acknowledges limitations

Principles

  • Plan before acting. Always write out your research plan before dispatching any agents.
  • Sequential, not parallel. Dispatch agents one at a time. Review each output before deciding what to do next. Later agents may need to account for what earlier agents found.
  • Adapt the plan. If an agent's findings reveal something unexpected or expose a gap, adjust the remaining plan. Document why.
  • Stay transparent. Every decision you make -- which agents to dispatch, what instructions to give them, how you adjusted the plan -- should be visible in the process log.
  • Acknowledge limits. If a sub-task cannot be adequately addressed, say so in the final report rather than papering over it.

Process Log Format

Maintain a running process log as you work. For each step, record:

`

Step [N]: [Brief description]

Agent type: Researcher / Analyst / Synthesiser

Task assigned: [The brief you gave the agent]

Key findings: [Summary of what came back]

Decision: [What you decided to do next, and why]

`

Output Format

When complete, produce two files in the project directory:

  • report.md -- the final research report
  • process_log.md -- the complete process log showing all steps, agent dispatches, and decisions

2. Key Concepts

Multi-Agent Orchestration

A single AI conversation -- even a powerful one -- is one model doing one thing at a time. Multi-agent orchestration changes this: a coordinating agent breaks a task into sub-tasks and dispatches specialist agents to handle each one. The orchestrator manages the workflow, passes outputs between agents, and assembles the final result.

This is not a metaphor. The orchestrator literally spawns separate AI instances, each with its own brief, and coordinates their work. The specialist agents do not see each other's instructions -- they receive only their own task and return their findings to the orchestrator.

The Orchestration Spec

The orchestrator's behaviour is defined by a specification file -- in this demo, a file called CLAUDE.md written in plain English. The spec describes:

  • The orchestrator's role and workflow (receive question, design plan, dispatch agents, collect results, synthesise)
  • The types of specialist agent available (Researcher, Analyst, Synthesiser) and what each does
  • Operating principles (plan before acting, sequential dispatch, adapt if needed, stay transparent)
  • Output requirements (a final report and a process log)

The spec is independent of the research question. It describes how to work, not what to work on. The question is provided separately at launch. This is exactly like writing standing instructions for a human research team.

The Claw Model

The demo in this session shows orchestration and multi-agent coordination -- the core of what makes agentic systems powerful. A full production implementation (sometimes called a "Claw") would go further:

  • Persistent state and memory -- the system maintains context across sessions, days, or weeks
  • Continuous execution loop -- observe, check for instructions, decide, act, update memory, repeat
  • Local environment access -- email, calendars, file systems, messaging apps, code repositories
  • Event-driven triggers -- the system responds to external events, not just user commands

We are not building or recommending a full Claw in this course. The security implications of a persistent AI agent with access to your local environment require careful institutional consideration. But the orchestration pattern demonstrated in this session is the foundation these systems are built on.

Overview

This guide describes a structured workflow for producing a major policy research report using AI tools. It is organised into a Setup Phase and three working phases, with dedicated evaluation checkpoints at the end of Phase 2 and Phase 3.

The workflow is designed for a two-person human team -- a subject lead and an AI engineer -- supported by a set of AI agents, each driven by its own agent instructions file. The subject lead owns all judgment calls. The AI engineer owns the agentic tooling. The agents handle bounded, repeatable tasks under clear instructions.

A companion reference table maps every step to its human roles and AI tools. Read the table first to orient yourself; use these descriptions when you need the reasoning behind a step.

Setup Phase -- Framing the Project

These two steps happen before any research begins. Their outputs -- the question map and the report specification -- are the project's constitution. They travel with the project, get embedded in agent instructions throughout, and provide the rubric against which every subsequent output is evaluated. Time spent here pays back at every later stage.

S1: Conceptual planning -- the question

Research briefs are written to commission work, not to guide it. Your first task is to translate the brief into a precise account of what a genuinely good answer would look like: the sub-questions that need answering, the domains that need covering, the evidence that would count as strong support for each. The output is a question map -- not long, but precise. An agent given a clear question map can evaluate its own output against explicit criteria. One given a vague brief cannot.

S2: Conceptual planning -- the report

The question map defines the intellectual requirements of a good answer. The report specification defines the practical requirements of a good deliverable: audience, purpose, structure, register, and what defensible looks like for this particular client. These are not the same thing, and conflating them produces reports that answer the question well but serve the client poorly -- or vice versa. Settle both before any research begins.

Phase 1 -- Data Gathering

Steps S3 and S4 run in parallel where the project allows.

S3a: Web search -- landscape pass

Submit your research brief to Claude Research with minimal direction. You are not trusting its output at this stage. You are using it to discover sources you might not have found independently, to see how the tool frames the problem, and to produce a first source list as raw material for curation. When the run completes, set the report aside and focus on the sources.

S3b: Web search -- source ranking

A source ranking agent works through the landscape pass output and scores each source against explicit criteria: recency, authority, geographic and sectoral specificity, and relevance to the research question. The output is a ranked list, not a curated one -- ranking is a mechanical task that an agent handles consistently; curation is a judgment call that requires domain knowledge.

S3c: Web search -- source curation

The subject lead reviews the ranked list and makes the curation decisions: which sources to prioritise, which to set aside, and which gaps need filling from their own knowledge of the domain. This step has no agent component. It is the most important quality gate in the entire workflow -- gaps in the source base cannot be fixed at the report writing stage -- and it belongs entirely to the human with domain expertise.

S3d: Web search -- directed extraction

A search agent works through the curated source list systematically, extracts relevant material against the research questions in the question map, and produces structured findings. The quality of this step depends directly on the quality of the agent instructions: a well-crafted brief produces consistent, usable output; a vague one produces summaries that require extensive reworking. This is where investment in the agent instructions pays off most visibly.

S4: Offline data processing

Offline material -- confidential documents, internal reports, messy spreadsheets -- often contains the most valuable evidence and requires the most careful handling. The governing principle is simple: material containing sensitive or confidential data should never enter a web-connected tool. A separate, isolated Project handles raw offline material only. Clean and anonymise before uploading; review all outputs before they leave the offline environment. Nothing passes directly from this Project to the main research Project without human review.

Phase 2 -- Processing

S5: Preliminary summaries

The web search and offline workstreams produce findings in different formats, at different levels of detail, organised around different principles. Preliminary summaries bring them to a common structure -- organised around the question map -- so they can be worked across in thematic synthesis. The human role here is review, not production: does each summary accurately represent what the source material contains, without adding interpretation the sources don't support?

S6: Thematic synthesis

This is the intellectual centre of the project. The preliminary summaries contain findings organised by source. Thematic synthesis reorganises them by argument -- across sources, across workstreams, in response to the research question. The output is not a summary of what the sources say. It is a structured account of what the evidence shows. The synthesis agent should be instructed to return to the question map, organise the evidence around it, and flag where coverage is thin or contradictory. The human review at this step is substantive -- the last analytical quality gate before evaluation.

Evaluation 1

E1: Grounding check

An evaluation agent works through the thematic synthesis and verifies that every substantive claim can be traced to a source in the preliminary summaries. Where a claim cannot be traced, the agent flags it explicitly. The critical instruction: flag, do not fix. An agent that silently patches gaps is more dangerous than one that surfaces them. The subject lead then resolves every flagged claim -- grounding it in the source material, revising it to reflect what the sources actually support, or removing it.

E2: Coverage and coherence

Once the grounding check is complete, the subject lead reads the thematic synthesis against the question map and the report specification. Is every sub-question addressed? Does the structure serve the intended audience? Are the limitations of the evidence honestly represented? This is a judgment call that no agent can make reliably. The project does not move to Phase 3 until both evaluation steps are complete.

Phase 3 -- Report Writing

S7: Final report

The thematic synthesis is organised around the research question. The final report is organised around the reader. The move from one to the other -- adjusting structure, register, and emphasis for the intended audience -- is the first task of this step. The writing agent works from the verified thematic synthesis and the report specification; its instructions should include an explicit directive to flag any point where the evidence feels thin or a claim feels stronger than the source material supports. The human role is editorial in the fullest sense: not just checking for errors but making the judgments about emphasis, framing, and tone that determine whether the report is genuinely useful to its audience.

Evaluation 2

E3: Final evaluation pass

One complete read of the finished report against all three rubric levels -- process quality, content quality, output quality -- before it leaves the team. Not to find new problems; the earlier checkpoints should have caught those. The purpose is to verify that the report as a whole is coherent, that the claims in the executive summary are supported by the body, and that the limitations are honestly stated. The question that drives this pass is the same one from Session 3: could you hand this report to a senior client and defend every claim in it? If the answer is yes, the report is ready.

Closing -- Assembling the Team

The workflow described in this guide requires two human roles and one that is increasingly a specialism in its own right.

The subject lead owns the Setup Phase, the curation decisions, all evaluation checkpoints, and the final report. Domain expertise is the qualification -- this is the person who knows which sources are authoritative, which claims are well-grounded, and what the client actually needs to hear.

The AI engineer builds and maintains the agent instructions files, runs the agentic steps, and adapts the playbooks to new client contexts. This is not a software engineering role -- it does not require coding expertise -- but it does require fluency with Claude Code, careful attention to instruction design, and the judgment to know when a process needs a full orchestrating agent and when a well-crafted Claude Chat instruction will do.

The agent instructions files developed across a project -- search, ranking, summarisation, synthesis, evaluation, writing -- are reusable assets. A team that refines them across multiple projects builds something genuinely valuable: a policy research capability that gets better with use, and that can be adapted to new domains and new clients without starting from scratch.

The minimum viable team for a serious policy research project is one subject lead and one AI engineer. On larger projects a coordination role may be needed; on smaller ones, a single person may cover both roles if they have both domain expertise and AI fluency. What the workflow cannot accommodate is the absence of either -- domain expertise without AI fluency produces research that doesn't use the tools well; AI fluency without domain expertise produces polished output that can't be defended.
